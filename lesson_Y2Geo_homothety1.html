Homothety: Part 1

Scaling Transformations and Their Properties

Introduction

Homothety is a fundamental geometric transformation that scales figures uniformly from a fixed center point. Unlike translations that move figures without changing their size, homotheties preserve shape but alter size while maintaining orientation (except when the ratio is negative).

In this lesson, we will explore the definition of homothety, learn to construct images under homothety, study their properties, and understand how homotheties relate to vector operations and barycenters.

Activity 2: Parallelogram Analysis

Let ABCD be a parallelogram, and let E and F be points such that \( \overrightarrow{AF} = 3\overrightarrow{AB} \) and \( \overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} \). Answer true or false, justifying your response.

Figure: Parallelogram ABCD with points E and F
Parallelogram ABCD with points E and F defined by vector relations.

a) Points A, D and E are collinear.

TRUE. Since \( \overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} \), vectors \( \overrightarrow{DE} \) and \( \overrightarrow{DA} \) are collinear. Therefore points D, E, and A are aligned. Alternatively, E lies on line (DA) but on the opposite side of A relative to D.

\( \overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} \) â‡’ \( \overrightarrow{DE} \parallel \overrightarrow{DA} \) â‡’ D, E, A collinear

b) Vectors \( \overrightarrow{AD} \) and \( \overrightarrow{BF} \) are collinear.

TRUE. In parallelogram ABCD, \( \overrightarrow{AD} = \overrightarrow{BC} \). Since \( \overrightarrow{AF} = 3\overrightarrow{AB} \), point F is such that \( \overrightarrow{BF} = \overrightarrow{BA} + \overrightarrow{AF} = -\overrightarrow{AB} + 3\overrightarrow{AB} = 2\overrightarrow{AB} \). But in parallelogram, \( \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC} \) which is parallel to \( \overrightarrow{AB} \). Actually, \( \overrightarrow{AD} = \overrightarrow{BC} \) and \( \overrightarrow{BF} = 2\overrightarrow{AB} \). Since ABCD is parallelogram, \( \overrightarrow{AD} \parallel \overrightarrow{BC} \) and \( \overrightarrow{AB} \parallel \overrightarrow{DC} \), but \( \overrightarrow{AD} \) is not necessarily parallel to \( \overrightarrow{AB} \). Wait, let's check carefully: \( \overrightarrow{BF} = \overrightarrow{BA} + \overrightarrow{AF} = -\overrightarrow{AB} + 3\overrightarrow{AB} = 2\overrightarrow{AB} \). So \( \overrightarrow{BF} \) is collinear with \( \overrightarrow{AB} \). \( \overrightarrow{AD} \) is collinear with \( \overrightarrow{BC} \). In a general parallelogram, \( \overrightarrow{AB} \) and \( \overrightarrow{AD} \) are not collinear (unless it's a degenerate parallelogram). So FALSE in general.

c) Vectors \( \overrightarrow{AB} \) and \( \overrightarrow{CD} \) are not collinear.

FALSE. In parallelogram ABCD, \( \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC} \), so \( \overrightarrow{AB} \) and \( \overrightarrow{CD} \) are opposite vectors: \( \overrightarrow{CD} = -\overrightarrow{AB} \). They are collinear (in fact, parallel and same line but opposite direction).

In parallelogram: \( \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC} \) â‡’ \( \overrightarrow{CD} = -\overrightarrow{AB} \) â‡’ collinear

d) Vectors \( 2\overrightarrow{CD} \) and \( \overrightarrow{AB} \) are collinear.

TRUE. As above, \( \overrightarrow{CD} = -\overrightarrow{AB} \), so \( 2\overrightarrow{CD} = -2\overrightarrow{AB} \), which is collinear with \( \overrightarrow{AB} \).

\( 2\overrightarrow{CD} = 2(-\overrightarrow{AB}) = -2\overrightarrow{AB} \) â‡’ collinear with \( \overrightarrow{AB} \)

e) Vectors \( \overrightarrow{EC} \) and \( \overrightarrow{EF} \) are collinear.

TRUE. Let's compute: \( \overrightarrow{EC} = \overrightarrow{ED} + \overrightarrow{DC} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} + \overrightarrow{DC} \) (since \( \overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} \), so \( \overrightarrow{ED} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} \)). And \( \overrightarrow{EF} = \overrightarrow{ED} + \overrightarrow{DF} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} + \overrightarrow{DF} \). But \( \overrightarrow{DF} = \overrightarrow{DA} + \overrightarrow{AF} = \overrightarrow{DA} + 3\overrightarrow{AB} \). Also \( \overrightarrow{DC} = \overrightarrow{AB} \). So \( \overrightarrow{EC} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} + \overrightarrow{AB} \) and \( \overrightarrow{EF} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} + \overrightarrow{DA} + 3\overrightarrow{AB} = \frac{3}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} + 3\overrightarrow{AB} = 3(\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} + \overrightarrow{AB}) = 3\overrightarrow{EC} \). Therefore they are collinear.

\( \overrightarrow{EF} = 3\overrightarrow{EC} \) â‡’ collinear

f) Points E, C and F are collinear.

TRUE. From part (e), \( \overrightarrow{EF} = 3\overrightarrow{EC} \), so vectors \( \overrightarrow{EC} \) and \( \overrightarrow{EF} \) are collinear, meaning points E, C, and F are aligned.

\( \overrightarrow{EF} = 3\overrightarrow{EC} \) â‡’ E, C, F collinear

Definition of Homothety

Let I be a point and k a non-zero real number. The application that associates to each point M in the plane the unique point M' such that \( \overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM} \) is called a homothety with center I and ratio k. It is denoted h(I, k).

h(I, k): P â†’ P
M â†¦ M' = h(I, k)(M) such that \( \overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM} \)

M' is called the image of point M by the homothety h(I, k).
M is called an antecedent (preimage) of M' by the homothety h(I, k).

Consequences

  • A point and its image are aligned with the center of the homothety.
  • \( h(I, k)(I) = I \). We say that I is an invariant point under h.
  • \( h(I, k)(M) = M' \) is equivalent to \( \overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM} \) which is equivalent to \( \overrightarrow{IM} = \frac{1}{k} \overrightarrow{IM'} \).
  • Every point in the plane has a unique antecedent under a homothety.

Part II: Construction of the Image of a Point by a Homothety

Activity 3: Recognizing a Transformation

Let A and B be two distinct points in the plane and let I be the barycenter of (A, 1) and (B, 4). Let M be a point in the plane and M' the point defined by \( \overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{MA} + 4\overrightarrow{MB} \). Recognize the application f of the plane that associates to point M the point M'.

Construction: Barycenter I of (A,1) and (B,4)
Points A and B with barycenter I. Point M with its image M' constructed according to the vector relation.

Let I be the barycenter of (A,1) and (B,4). By definition of barycenter: \( \overrightarrow{IA} + 4\overrightarrow{IB} = \vec{0} \), or equivalently \( \overrightarrow{AI} + 4\overrightarrow{BI} = \vec{0} \).

We have \( \overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{MA} + 4\overrightarrow{MB} \). Using Chasles relation: \( \overrightarrow{MA} = \overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IA} \) and \( \overrightarrow{MB} = \overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IB} \).

\( \overrightarrow{MM'} = (\overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IA}) + 4(\overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IB}) \)
\( \overrightarrow{MM'} = 5\overrightarrow{MI} + (\overrightarrow{IA} + 4\overrightarrow{IB}) \)

But \( \overrightarrow{IA} + 4\overrightarrow{IB} = \vec{0} \) from barycenter definition. So:

\( \overrightarrow{MM'} = 5\overrightarrow{MI} \)

Therefore \( \overrightarrow{IM'} = \overrightarrow{IM} + \overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{IM} + 5\overrightarrow{MI} = \overrightarrow{IM} - 5\overrightarrow{IM} = -4\overrightarrow{IM} \).

This shows that M' is the image of M by the homothety with center I and ratio -4: \( f = h(I, -4) \).

Part III: Characteristic Property of Homothety

Activity 4: Image of a Segment

Let h be a homothety with center I and ratio k, and let M and N be two points in the plane. Denote M' = h(M) and N' = h(N). Express \( \overrightarrow{M'N'} \) in terms of \( \overrightarrow{MN} \).

Homothety: Points M, N and their images M', N'
Points M and N with their images M' and N' under homothety with center I and ratio k.

By definition of homothety: \( \overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM} \) and \( \overrightarrow{IN'} = k \overrightarrow{IN} \).

We want \( \overrightarrow{M'N'} \). Using Chasles relation:

\( \overrightarrow{M'N'} = \overrightarrow{M'I} + \overrightarrow{IN'} = -\overrightarrow{IM'} + \overrightarrow{IN'} \)
\( \overrightarrow{M'N'} = -k \overrightarrow{IM} + k \overrightarrow{IN} = k(\overrightarrow{IN} - \overrightarrow{IM}) \)
\( \overrightarrow{M'N'} = k \overrightarrow{MN} \)

Thus, the homothety transforms vectors by multiplying them by the ratio k.

Consequences

If A' and B' are the respective images of two distinct points A and B under a homothety of ratio k, then:

  • Line (AB) is parallel to line (A'B');
  • \( A'B' = |k| \times AB \).

The absolute value |k| gives the scaling factor for distances, while the sign of k determines whether the image is on the same side (k > 0) or opposite side (k < 0) of the center relative to the original point.

Comprehensive Summary

Definition of Homothety

Given a point I (the center) and a non-zero real number k (the ratio), the homothety h(I, k) is the transformation that maps each point M to the unique point M' such that:

\( \overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM} \)

Equivalently: \( h(I, k)(M) = M' \) â‡” \( \overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM} \)

Key Properties

  • The center I is invariant: \( h(I, k)(I) = I \)
  • A point and its image are collinear with the center
  • The transformation is bijective: every point has a unique image and a unique preimage
  • For any two points M and N with images M' and N': \( \overrightarrow{M'N'} = k \overrightarrow{MN} \)
  • Lines are transformed to parallel lines: (AB) âˆ¥ (A'B')
  • Distances are scaled by |k|: \( A'B' = |k| \times AB \)
  • Angles are preserved (homothety is a similarity transformation)
  • When k > 0, the image is on the same side of the center as the original
  • When k < 0, the image is on the opposite side of the center

Key Relationship with Vectors

If I is the barycenter of (A, Î±) and (B, Î²), then the transformation defined by \( \overrightarrow{MM'} = Î± \overrightarrow{MA} + Î² \overrightarrow{MB} \) is a homothety with center I and ratio -(Î±+Î²):

\( \overrightarrow{MM'} = Î± \overrightarrow{MA} + Î² \overrightarrow{MB} \) â‡’ \( f = h(I, -(Î±+Î²)) \)

Homothety is a fundamental similarity transformation that scales figures uniformly from a center point. It preserves angles and shapes while altering sizes, making it essential in geometry, architecture, and design. Understanding homothety provides insight into proportional reasoning and similarity, connecting geometric transformations with algebraic concepts of vectors and ratios.
