Homothety: Part 1

Properties and Applications

Introduction

Homothety is a geometric transformation that enlarges or reduces figures while preserving their shape. It's a fundamental concept in geometry with applications in similarity, scaling, and geometric constructions.

In this lesson, we will explore the definition, properties, and constructions related to homothety through a series of exercises.

Recall Exercise

1) In the figure below, A and B are two distinct points on line (xy). Let k be a real number and let M be the point defined by \(\overrightarrow{AM} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\). Determine the position of point M on line (xy) in each of the following cases:

Figure: Points A and B on line (xy)
Points A and B on line (xy). M is defined by \(\overrightarrow{AM} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\).

a) k < 0

When k < 0, \(\overrightarrow{AM}\) has opposite direction to \(\overrightarrow{AB}\). Therefore, point M lies on line (AB) but on the opposite side of A relative to B.

\(\overrightarrow{AM} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\) with k < 0 â‡’ M is on line (AB) on the side opposite to B

b) k âˆˆ [0, 1]

When 0 â‰¤ k â‰¤ 1, point M lies on segment [AB]. Specifically:

  • If k = 0, then M = A
  • If k = 1, then M = B
  • If 0 < k < 1, M is between A and B

c) k > 1

When k > 1, \(\overrightarrow{AM}\) has the same direction as \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) but is longer. Therefore, point M lies on line (AB) beyond point B.

\(\overrightarrow{AM} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\) with k > 1 â‡’ M is on line (AB) beyond B

Activity 2: Parallelogram Analysis

Let ABCD be a parallelogram and E and F points such that \(\overrightarrow{AF} = 3\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA}\). Answer true or false, justifying your answer.

Figure: Parallelogram ABCD with points E and F
Parallelogram ABCD with \(\overrightarrow{AF} = 3\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA}\).

a) Points A, D, and E are collinear.

TRUE. Since \(\overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA}\), vectors \(\overrightarrow{DE}\) and \(\overrightarrow{DA}\) are collinear. Therefore, points D, E, and A are on the same line.

\(\overrightarrow{DE} = -\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{DA} \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{DE} \parallel \overrightarrow{DA} \Rightarrow D, E, A \text{ collinear}\)

b) Vectors \(\overrightarrow{AD}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BF}\) are collinear.

TRUE. In parallelogram ABCD, \(\overrightarrow{AD} = \overrightarrow{BC}\). Since \(\overrightarrow{AF} = 3\overrightarrow{AB}\), point F lies on line (AB) beyond B. Thus \(\overrightarrow{BF} = 2\overrightarrow{AB}\), which is parallel to \(\overrightarrow{AB}\). Since \(\overrightarrow{AD} \parallel \overrightarrow{BC} \parallel \overrightarrow{AB}\), vectors \(\overrightarrow{AD}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BF}\) are collinear.

\(\overrightarrow{AD} = \overrightarrow{BC} \parallel \overrightarrow{AB} \parallel \overrightarrow{BF} \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{AD} \parallel \overrightarrow{BF}\)

c) Vectors \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{CD}\) are not collinear.

FALSE. In parallelogram ABCD, opposite sides are parallel: \(\overrightarrow{AB} \parallel \overrightarrow{DC}\) (or \(\overrightarrow{CD}\)). Therefore, vectors \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{CD}\) are collinear (in fact, \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC}\)).

In parallelogram: \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC} \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{AB} \parallel \overrightarrow{CD}\)

d) Vectors \(2\overrightarrow{CD}\) and \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) are collinear.

TRUE. As established, \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC}\). Therefore, \(2\overrightarrow{CD} = -2\overrightarrow{DC} = -2\overrightarrow{AB}\). Since one vector is a scalar multiple of the other (\(-2\)), they are collinear.

\(2\overrightarrow{CD} = -2\overrightarrow{DC} = -2\overrightarrow{AB} \Rightarrow 2\overrightarrow{CD} \parallel \overrightarrow{AB}\)

e) Vectors \(\overrightarrow{EC}\) and \(\overrightarrow{EF}\) are collinear.

FALSE. We need to check if these vectors are parallel. Let's express them in terms of known vectors. Since E is on line DA, and C and F are fixed, generally these vectors are not collinear unless specific conditions hold (which they don't in a general parallelogram).

f) Points E, C, and F are collinear.

FALSE. Generally, E lies on line DA, C is a vertex of the parallelogram, and F lies on line AB extended. These three points are not collinear in a general parallelogram configuration.

Definition of Homothety

Let I be a point and k a non-zero real number. The transformation that maps every point M in the plane to the unique point M' such that \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM}\) is called a homothety with center I and ratio k. It is denoted by h(I, k).

h(I, k): P â†’ P
M â†¦ M' = h(I, k)(M) such that \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM}\)

M' is called the image of point M by the homothety h(I, k).
M is called an antecedent (or preimage) of M' by the homothety h(I, k).

Consequences

  • A point and its image are aligned with the center of the homothety.
  • h(I, k)(I) = I. We say that I is a fixed point (invariant point) of h.
  • h(I, k)(M) = M' â‡” \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM}\) â‡” \(\overrightarrow{IM} = \frac{1}{k} \overrightarrow{IM'}\).
  • Every point in the plane has a unique antecedent under a homothety.

Part II: Construction of the Image of a Point by a Homothety

Activity 3: Recognizing a Transformation

Let A and B be two distinct points in the plane and let I be the barycenter of (A, 1) and (B, 4). Let M be a point in the plane and M' the point defined by \(\overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{MA} + 4\overrightarrow{MB}\). Identify the transformation f of the plane that maps point M to point M'.

Figure: Points A, B, I (barycenter) and M, M'
I is barycenter of (A,1) and (B,4). M' satisfies \(\overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{MA} + 4\overrightarrow{MB}\).

First, note that I being the barycenter of (A,1) and (B,4) means:

\(\overrightarrow{IA} + 4\overrightarrow{IB} = \overrightarrow{0}\) or \(\overrightarrow{AI} = \frac{4}{5}\overrightarrow{AB}\)

Now, rewrite \(\overrightarrow{MM'}\):

\(\overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{MA} + 4\overrightarrow{MB}\)

Using Chasles relation, express in terms of I:

\(\overrightarrow{MA} = \overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IA}\)
\(\overrightarrow{MB} = \overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IB}\)

Thus:

\(\overrightarrow{MM'} = (\overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IA}) + 4(\overrightarrow{MI} + \overrightarrow{IB}) = 5\overrightarrow{MI} + (\overrightarrow{IA} + 4\overrightarrow{IB})\)

But \(\overrightarrow{IA} + 4\overrightarrow{IB} = \overrightarrow{0}\) by definition of I as barycenter. Therefore:

\(\overrightarrow{MM'} = 5\overrightarrow{MI}\)

This shows that \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = \overrightarrow{IM} + \overrightarrow{MM'} = \overrightarrow{IM} + 5\overrightarrow{MI} = \overrightarrow{IM} - 5\overrightarrow{IM} = -4\overrightarrow{IM}\).

Thus \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = -4\overrightarrow{IM}\), which means f is the homothety with center I and ratio -4:

f = h(I, -4)

Part III: Characteristic Property of Homothety

Activity 4: Distance Transformation

Let h be a homothety with center I and ratio k, and let M and N be two points in the plane. Denote M' = h(M) and N' = h(N). Express \(\overrightarrow{M'N'}\) in terms of \(\overrightarrow{MN}\).

Figure: Homothety transforming points M and N to M' and N'
Points M and N transformed by homothety h(I,k) to M' and N'.

By definition of homothety:

\(\overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM}\) and \(\overrightarrow{IN'} = k \overrightarrow{IN}\)

Now compute \(\overrightarrow{M'N'}\):

\(\overrightarrow{M'N'} = \overrightarrow{M'I} + \overrightarrow{IN'} = -\overrightarrow{IM'} + \overrightarrow{IN'}\)
\(\overrightarrow{M'N'} = -k \overrightarrow{IM} + k \overrightarrow{IN} = k(\overrightarrow{IN} - \overrightarrow{IM}) = k \overrightarrow{MN}\)

Therefore, \(\overrightarrow{M'N'} = k \overrightarrow{MN}\).

Consequences

If A' and B' are the respective images of two distinct points A and B under a homothety with ratio k, then:

  • Line (AB) is parallel to line (A'B')
  • A'B' = |k| Ã— AB (the distance is multiplied by the absolute value of k)
\(\overrightarrow{A'B'} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(A'B' = |k| \times AB\)

Comprehensive Summary

Definition of Homothety

A homothety with center I and non-zero ratio k is the transformation h(I, k) that maps any point M to the unique point M' such that:

h(I, k)(M) = M' â‡” \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = k \overrightarrow{IM}\)

Key Properties

  • The center I is a fixed point: h(I, k)(I) = I
  • A point and its image are aligned with the center
  • For any points M, N with images M', N': \(\overrightarrow{M'N'} = k \overrightarrow{MN}\)
  • Lines are transformed to parallel lines: (AB) âˆ¥ (A'B')
  • Distances are multiplied by |k|: A'B' = |k| Ã— AB
  • Every point has a unique antecedent under a homothety
