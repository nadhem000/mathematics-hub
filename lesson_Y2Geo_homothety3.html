Homothety: Part 3

Exercises and Problems

Introduction

In this lesson, we will apply homothety concepts to solve various types of problems. The exercises cover construction problems, alignment proofs, identification of homothetic figures, and properties preservation under homothety.

Through these exercises, you will develop your skills in recognizing homotheties, constructing images, and proving geometric properties using homothety transformations.

IX. Construction Problem

Activity 1

Let D and Œî be two intersecting lines. Let I be a point belonging neither to D nor to Œî.

  1. a) Construct the line D', image of D by the homothety h with center I and ratio 2.
    Let M' be the intersection point of lines D' and Œî.
  2. b) Construct the point M, antecedent of point M' by h.
  3. Construct a point A on D and a point B on Œî such that \(\overrightarrow{IB} = 3\overrightarrow{IA}\).
Figure: Lines D and Œî intersecting, point I not on either line
Construct line D' = h(I,2)(D), find M' = D'‚à©Œî, then find M = h‚Åª¬π(M'). Construct A on D and B on Œî such that IB = 3IA.

Construction Steps

  1. To construct D' = h(I,2)(D):
    • Choose two distinct points P and Q on line D.
    • Construct their images P' and Q' by homothety h(I,2): Draw rays IP and IQ, then mark P' and Q' such that IP' = 2√óIP and IQ' = 2√óIQ.
    • Line D' is the line passing through P' and Q'.
  2. M' is the intersection of D' and Œî.
  3. To find M = h‚Åª¬π(M'):
    • Since h has ratio 2, its inverse h‚Åª¬π has ratio 1/2.
    • Construct M on line IM' such that IM = (1/2)IM' (or equivalently, M' is between I and M with IM = (1/2)IM').
  4. To construct A on D and B on Œî with \(\overrightarrow{IB} = 3\overrightarrow{IA}\):
    • Choose any point A on line D.
    • Construct B as the image of A by homothety h(I,3): Draw ray IA and mark B such that IB = 3√óIA.
    • Since B must lie on Œî, adjust A along D until B falls on Œî.
Key relations: \(\overrightarrow{IM'} = 2\overrightarrow{IM}\) and \(\overrightarrow{IB} = 3\overrightarrow{IA}\)

X. Alignment Problem

Activity 2

Let C and C' be two circles with respective centers O and O', intersecting at points A and B. Let I be the intersection point of lines (AB) and (OO').

  1. Show that point I is the midpoint of segment [OO'].
  2. Line (AO) meets circle C again at E, and line (AO') meets circle C' again at F.
    Show that points E, B, and F are collinear.
  3. Line (BO) meets circle C again at G, and line (BO') meets circle C' again at H.
    Show that quadrilateral EFGH is a rectangle.
Figure: Two circles C and C' intersecting at A and B
Circles with centers O and O', intersecting at A and B. I = (AB)‚à©(OO'). Points E, F, G, H as described.

Solution

  1. Proof that I is midpoint of [OO']:
    • Consider homothety h with center A that maps circle C to circle C'.
    • Since A is common to both circles, the homothety maps A to itself.
    • Point B on C maps to B on C' (since B is common).
    • Center O of C maps to center O' of C'. Therefore, A, O, O' are collinear.
    • Similarly, consider homothety with center B: B, O, O' are collinear.
    • Thus line OO' passes through both A and B, so OO' = AB, and I = AB ‚à© OO' is indeed the intersection.
    • Since the two homotheties (with centers A and B) are inverses, I is the midpoint of OO'.
    \(\overrightarrow{O'I} = -\overrightarrow{OI}\) or \(I = \text{midpoint}(OO')\)
  2. Proof that E, B, F are collinear:
    • Consider homothety h‚ÇÅ with center A mapping C to C'.
    • Under h‚ÇÅ: O ‚Üí O', and the line AO (diameter of C) maps to line AO' (diameter of C').
    • Point E (intersection of AO with C, other than A) maps to point F (intersection of AO' with C', other than A).
    • Since B is fixed point of h‚ÇÅ (as common point), and E, B, F are aligned with the center A, they are collinear.
    \(h‚ÇÅ(A,k): E \mapsto F\) with \(A, E, B, F\) collinear
  3. Proof that EFGH is a rectangle:
    • Similarly, consider homothety h‚ÇÇ with center B mapping C to C'.
    • Under h‚ÇÇ: O ‚Üí O', and line BO maps to line BO'.
    • Point G (intersection of BO with C, other than B) maps to point H (intersection of BO' with C', other than B).
    • Now, consider quadrilateral EFGH:
      • EF is diameter of circle through A (since AE is diameter of C and AF is diameter of C').
      • GH is diameter of circle through B (since BG is diameter of C and BH is diameter of C').
      • Since AB is common chord and OO' is line of centers, and I is midpoint of OO', we have symmetry.
      • Angles E and G are right angles (angles in semicircles).
      • Similarly, angles F and H are right angles.
    • Thus EFGH has four right angles, so it is a rectangle.
    \(\widehat{E} = \widehat{F} = \widehat{G} = \widehat{H} = 90^\circ\) ‚áí EFGH is a rectangle

Definition

Analysis

To determine if a figure is homothetic to F:

  1. Check shape preservation: All angles must be preserved. Corresponding sides must be parallel.
  2. Check scaling factor: The ratio of corresponding lengths must be constant (same for all pairs).
  3. Check center existence: Lines joining corresponding points must be concurrent at a single center point.

XII. Conservation of Angles

Activity 4

Let h be a homothety with center O and ratio k. Let ABC be a triangle.

  1. Construct triangle A'B'C', image of ABC by h.
  2. a) Construct triangle A'B''C'', image of ABC by the translation with vector \(\overrightarrow{AA'}\).
    b) Show that triangles ABC and A'B''C'' are isometric (congruent).
  3. Deduce that \(\widehat{BAC} = \widehat{B'A'C'}\).
    What can we conclude?
Figure: Triangle ABC and its images by homothety and translation
Triangle ABC, its homothetic image A'B'C' (center O, ratio k), and translated image A'B''C'' (vector AA').

Solution

  1. Construction of A'B'C':
    • Draw rays OA, OB, OC.
    • Mark A', B', C' on these rays such that OA' = k√óOA, OB' = k√óOB, OC' = k√óOC.
    • Connect A', B', C' to form triangle A'B'C'.
  2. Construction of A'B''C'':
    • The translation with vector \(\overrightarrow{AA'}\) maps A to A'.
    • Apply the same translation to B and C to get B'' and C'' such that \(\overrightarrow{AB''} = \overrightarrow{AA'} + \overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{AC''} = \overrightarrow{AA'} + \overrightarrow{AC}\).
  3. Proof that ABC ‚âÖ A'B''C'':
    • By definition of translation: \(\overrightarrow{AA'} = \overrightarrow{BB''} = \overrightarrow{CC''}\).
    • Therefore, AB = A'B'', BC = B''C'', CA = C''A' (translation preserves distances).
    • Thus triangles ABC and A'B''C'' have equal corresponding sides, so they are congruent (isometric).
    \(\overrightarrow{AA'} = \overrightarrow{BB''} = \overrightarrow{CC''}\) ‚áí AB = A'B'', BC = B''C'', CA = C''A'
  4. Deduction that \(\widehat{BAC} = \widehat{B'A'C'}\):
    • Since ABC ‚âÖ A'B''C'', we have \(\widehat{BAC} = \widehat{B''A'C''}\).
    • But A'B''C'' is obtained from A'B'C' by translation (or is parallel to it).
    • Actually, B''C'' is parallel to B'C' (since both are parallel to BC).
    • Thus \(\widehat{B''A'C''} = \widehat{B'A'C'}\) (corresponding angles with parallel lines).
    \(\widehat{BAC} = \widehat{B''A'C''} = \widehat{B'A'C'}\)

Conclusion: A homothety preserves angles. More generally, homotheties are angle-preserving transformations (they are conformal).

XIII. Conservation of Parallelism and Orthogonality

Activity 5

Let h be a homothety with center I and ratio k. Let D and Œî be two lines, and let D' and Œî' be their respective images by h. What can we say about D' and Œî' in each of the following cases:

  1. a) D and Œî are parallel?
  2. b) D and Œî are perpendicular?
Figure: Lines D and Œî and their homothetic images D' and Œî'
Show cases: parallel lines remain parallel, perpendicular lines remain perpendicular under homothety.

Solution

  1. Case a: D ‚à• Œî
    • Let D and Œî be two parallel lines.
    • Take two points A and B on D, and two points C and D on Œî (with AB parallel to CD).
    • Their images A', B', C', D' satisfy: \(\overrightarrow{A'B'} = k\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{C'D'} = k\overrightarrow{CD}\).
    • Since \(\overrightarrow{AB} \parallel \overrightarrow{CD}\), we have \(\overrightarrow{A'B'} \parallel \overrightarrow{C'D'}\).
    • Thus D' (line through A' and B') is parallel to Œî' (line through C' and D').
    \(D \parallel Œî \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{AB} \parallel \overrightarrow{CD} \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{A'B'} \parallel \overrightarrow{C'D'} \Rightarrow D' \parallel Œî'\)
  2. Case b: D ‚üÇ Œî
    • Let D and Œî be two perpendicular lines (D ‚üÇ Œî).
    • Take two points A and B on D, and two points C and D on Œî such that AB ‚üÇ CD.
    • From Activity 4, we know homothety preserves angles.
    • Specifically, the angle between lines D and Œî is preserved.
    • Thus D' ‚üÇ Œî' (the image lines remain perpendicular).
    \(D \perp Œî \Rightarrow \widehat{(D,Œî)} = 90^\circ \Rightarrow \widehat{(D',Œî')} = 90^\circ \Rightarrow D' \perp Œî'\)
General Conclusion

Homothety preserves:

  • Parallelism (parallel lines map to parallel lines)
  • Angles (including right angles, so perpendicularity is preserved)
  • Shape (figures remain similar)
  • Orientation if k > 0, reversed if k < 0

Comprehensive Summary

Key Properties Demonstrated

  • Use of homothety to prove alignment and midpoint properties
  • Recognition of homothetic figures (preservation of shape)
  • Homothety preserves angles (is conformal)
  • Homothety preserves parallelism and perpendicularity

Conclusion

Through these exercises, we have explored the practical applications of homothety in solving geometric problems. Homothety is not just a theoretical concept but a powerful tool for constructions, proofs, and transformations. The preservation of angles, parallelism, and shape under homothety makes it fundamental to the study of similarity in geometry. Mastery of these concepts provides a strong foundation for more advanced geometric transformations and their applications in various fields including engineering, architecture, and computer graphics.
