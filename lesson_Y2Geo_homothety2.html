Homothety: Part 2

Properties and Applications - Exercises

Introduction

This second part of the homothety lesson focuses on important properties and applications through exercises. We will explore how homotheties preserve alignment, transform lines and segments, conserve midpoints and barycenters, and analyze specific geometric configurations.

Through these exercises, you will master the fundamental properties of homotheties and learn to apply them to solve complex geometric problems.

I. Conservation of Alignment

Activity 1: Alignment Preservation

Let h be a homothety with center I and ratio k. Let A, B, and C be three aligned points, and A', B', and C' their respective images under h. Show that points A', B', and C' are aligned.

Figure: Homothety preserves alignment
Points A, B, C are aligned. Their images A', B', C' under homothety h(I,k) remain aligned.

Proof:

Since A, B, and C are aligned, there exists a real number Œª such that:

\(\overrightarrow{AC} = Œª \overrightarrow{AB}\)

By definition of homothety h with center I and ratio k:

\(\overrightarrow{IA'} = k \overrightarrow{IA}, \quad \overrightarrow{IB'} = k \overrightarrow{IB}, \quad \overrightarrow{IC'} = k \overrightarrow{IC}\)

We need to show that A', B', and C' are aligned, i.e., there exists a real number Œº such that:

\(\overrightarrow{A'C'} = Œº \overrightarrow{A'B'}\)

Compute \(\overrightarrow{A'C'}\):

\(\overrightarrow{A'C'} = \overrightarrow{A'I} + \overrightarrow{IC'} = -\overrightarrow{IA'} + \overrightarrow{IC'} = -k \overrightarrow{IA} + k \overrightarrow{IC} = k(\overrightarrow{IC} - \overrightarrow{IA}) = k \overrightarrow{AC}\)

Similarly, compute \(\overrightarrow{A'B'}\):

\(\overrightarrow{A'B'} = \overrightarrow{A'I} + \overrightarrow{IB'} = -\overrightarrow{IA'} + \overrightarrow{IB'} = -k \overrightarrow{IA} + k \overrightarrow{IB} = k(\overrightarrow{IB} - \overrightarrow{IA}) = k \overrightarrow{AB}\)

Therefore:

\(\overrightarrow{A'C'} = k \overrightarrow{AC} = k(Œª \overrightarrow{AB}) = Œª (k \overrightarrow{AB}) = Œª \overrightarrow{A'B'}\)

Thus, \(\overrightarrow{A'C'} = Œª \overrightarrow{A'B'}\), which proves that A', B', and C' are aligned.

Conclusion: Homothety preserves alignment of points.

II. Image of a Line - Image of a Segment

Activity 2: Line and Segment Transformation

Let h be a homothety with center I and ratio k. Let A and B be two distinct points in the plane. Denote by A' and B' their respective images under h.

Figure: Homothety transforms lines to parallel lines
Line (AB) and its image (A'B') under homothety h(I,k).

1) a) Let M be a point in the plane and M' its image under h.

Express vectorially the statement: "M belongs to line (AB)". Show that if M belongs to line (AB) then M' belongs to line (A'B').

a) Solution:

M belongs to line (AB) if and only if vectors \(\overrightarrow{AM}\) and \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) are collinear:

\(\overrightarrow{AM} = t \overrightarrow{AB}\) for some real number t

Now, suppose M belongs to line (AB). We need to show M' belongs to line (A'B'), i.e., \(\overrightarrow{A'M'}\) is collinear with \(\overrightarrow{A'B'}\).

Using the property of homothety:

\(\overrightarrow{A'M'} = k \overrightarrow{AM} = k(t \overrightarrow{AB}) = t(k \overrightarrow{AB}) = t \overrightarrow{A'B'}\)

Thus, \(\overrightarrow{A'M'} = t \overrightarrow{A'B'}\), which means M' belongs to line (A'B').

b) Let N' be a point on (A'B') and N its antecedent under h.

Show that N belongs to line (AB).

b) Solution:

Since N' belongs to line (A'B'), we have:

\(\overrightarrow{A'N'} = s \overrightarrow{A'B'}\) for some real number s

But \(\overrightarrow{A'N'} = k \overrightarrow{AN}\) and \(\overrightarrow{A'B'} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\), so:

\(k \overrightarrow{AN} = s(k \overrightarrow{AB}) \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{AN} = s \overrightarrow{AB}\)

Thus, N belongs to line (AB).

c) What can we conclude?

c) Conclusion:

From parts (a) and (b), we conclude that the image of line (AB) under homothety h is exactly line (A'B').

\(h((AB)) = (A'B')\)

d) What is the relative position of lines (AB) and (A'B')?

d) Solution:

Since \(\overrightarrow{A'B'} = k \overrightarrow{AB}\), vectors \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{A'B'}\) are collinear.

Therefore, lines (AB) and (A'B') are parallel.

\((AB) \parallel (A'B')\)

2) Show that the image of segment [AB] under h is the segment [A'B'].

2) Solution:

Let M be a point on segment [AB]. Then there exists t ‚àà [0, 1] such that:

\(\overrightarrow{AM} = t \overrightarrow{AB}\)

Its image M' satisfies:

\(\overrightarrow{A'M'} = k \overrightarrow{AM} = k(t \overrightarrow{AB}) = t(k \overrightarrow{AB}) = t \overrightarrow{A'B'}\)

Since t ‚àà [0, 1], M' belongs to segment [A'B'].

Conversely, if N' belongs to segment [A'B'], then \(\overrightarrow{A'N'} = s \overrightarrow{A'B'}\) with s ‚àà [0, 1]. Its antecedent N satisfies:

\(\overrightarrow{AN} = s \overrightarrow{AB}\)

Thus N belongs to segment [AB].

Conclusion: \(h([AB]) = [A'B']\)

III. Conservation of Midpoint

Activity 3: Midpoint Preservation

1) In the opposite figure, I is the midpoint of segment [AB], A' is the image of A under a homothety with center O.

a) Copy the figure and construct points I' and B' respective images of I and B under this homothety.

b) What does I' represent for segment [A'B']?

Figure: Homothety preserves midpoints
I is midpoint of [AB]. Under homothety h(O,k), I' is midpoint of [A'B'].

1) a) Construction

a) Construction:

Given A' = h(A), we construct:

  • B' such that \(\overrightarrow{OB'} = k \overrightarrow{OB}\)
  • I' such that \(\overrightarrow{OI'} = k \overrightarrow{OI}\)

Since I is the midpoint of [AB], we have \(\overrightarrow{AI} = \frac{1}{2} \overrightarrow{AB}\).

b) What does I' represent for segment [A'B']?

b) Solution:

We need to show that I' is the midpoint of [A'B'].

Since I is midpoint of [AB]:

\(\overrightarrow{AI} = \overrightarrow{IB} \quad \text{or} \quad \overrightarrow{IA} + \overrightarrow{IB} = \overrightarrow{0}\)

Now consider I' and segment [A'B']:

\(\overrightarrow{A'I'} = k \overrightarrow{AI} \quad \text{and} \quad \overrightarrow{I'B'} = k \overrightarrow{IB}\)
\(\overrightarrow{A'I'} = k \overrightarrow{AI} = k \overrightarrow{IB} = \overrightarrow{I'B'}\)

Thus, \(\overrightarrow{A'I'} = \overrightarrow{I'B'}\), so I' is the midpoint of [A'B'].

Conclusion: I' is the midpoint of segment [A'B'].

2) General property

Let h be a homothety with ratio k. Let [MN] be a segment and P its midpoint. Denote by M', N', and P' the respective images of M, N, and P under h. Show that P' is the midpoint of [M'N'].

2) General Proof:

Since P is the midpoint of [MN], we have:

\(\overrightarrow{MP} = \overrightarrow{PN} \quad \text{or} \quad \overrightarrow{MN} = 2 \overrightarrow{MP}\)

Now consider the images:

\(\overrightarrow{M'P'} = k \overrightarrow{MP} \quad \text{and} \quad \overrightarrow{P'N'} = k \overrightarrow{PN}\)

Since \(\overrightarrow{MP} = \overrightarrow{PN}\), we have:

\(\overrightarrow{M'P'} = k \overrightarrow{MP} = k \overrightarrow{PN} = \overrightarrow{P'N'}\)

Therefore, \(\overrightarrow{M'P'} = \overrightarrow{P'N'}\), which means P' is the midpoint of [M'N'].

Conclusion: Homothety preserves midpoints.

IV. Conservation of Barycenter

Activity 4: Barycenter Preservation

Let h be a homothety with ratio k.

Figure: Homothety preserves barycenters
Barycenter G of weighted points (A,Œ±), (B,Œ≤). Its image G' is barycenter of (A',Œ±), (B',Œ≤).

1) Let Œ± and Œ≤ be two real numbers such that Œ± + Œ≤ ‚â† 0.

Let M and N be two points in the plane. Denote by M' and N' their respective images under h. Let P be the barycenter of points (M, Œ±) and (N, Œ≤) and P' the image of P under h. Show that P' is the barycenter of points (M', Œ±) and (N', Œ≤).

1) Proof:

Let O be an arbitrary point (we can take O as the center of homothety h).

Since P is the barycenter of (M, Œ±) and (N, Œ≤), we have:

\(Œ± \overrightarrow{OP} + Œ≤ \overrightarrow{OP} = Œ± \overrightarrow{OM} + Œ≤ \overrightarrow{ON}\)
\((Œ± + Œ≤) \overrightarrow{OP} = Œ± \overrightarrow{OM} + Œ≤ \overrightarrow{ON}\)

Apply the homothety h with center I and ratio k. For any point X with image X':

\(\overrightarrow{IX'} = k \overrightarrow{IX} \quad \text{or} \quad \overrightarrow{OX'} = \overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OX} - \overrightarrow{OI})\)

Substitute this into the barycenter equation:

\((Œ± + Œ≤) \overrightarrow{OP'} = (Œ± + Œ≤)(\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OP} - \overrightarrow{OI}))\)
\(= (Œ± + Œ≤)\overrightarrow{OI} + k(Œ± + Œ≤)(\overrightarrow{OP} - \overrightarrow{OI})\)
\(= (Œ± + Œ≤)\overrightarrow{OI} + k(Œ± \overrightarrow{OM} + Œ≤ \overrightarrow{ON} - (Œ± + Œ≤)\overrightarrow{OI})\)
\(= (Œ± + Œ≤)\overrightarrow{OI} + Œ± k(\overrightarrow{OM} - \overrightarrow{OI}) + Œ≤ k(\overrightarrow{ON} - \overrightarrow{OI})\)
\(= Œ±(\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OM} - \overrightarrow{OI})) + Œ≤(\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{ON} - \overrightarrow{OI}))\)
\(= Œ± \overrightarrow{OM'} + Œ≤ \overrightarrow{ON'}\)

Thus, \((Œ± + Œ≤) \overrightarrow{OP'} = Œ± \overrightarrow{OM'} + Œ≤ \overrightarrow{ON'}\), which means P' is the barycenter of (M', Œ±) and (N', Œ≤).

2) Let A, B, and C be three points in the plane and G the barycenter of points (A, 2), (B, -1), and (C, 3).

Denote by A', B', C', and G' the respective images of A, B, C, and G under h. Show that G' is the barycenter of points (A', 2), (B', -1), and (C', 3).

2) Proof:

This is a direct application of the general result from part 1.

Let O be an arbitrary point. Since G is barycenter of (A,2), (B,-1), (C,3):

\((2 - 1 + 3) \overrightarrow{OG} = 2 \overrightarrow{OA} - \overrightarrow{OB} + 3 \overrightarrow{OC}\)
\(4 \overrightarrow{OG} = 2 \overrightarrow{OA} - \overrightarrow{OB} + 3 \overrightarrow{OC}\)

Apply the same transformation as in part 1:

\(4 \overrightarrow{OG'} = 4(\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OG} - \overrightarrow{OI}))\)
\(= 4\overrightarrow{OI} + k(4\overrightarrow{OG} - 4\overrightarrow{OI})\)
\(= 4\overrightarrow{OI} + k(2\overrightarrow{OA} - \overrightarrow{OB} + 3\overrightarrow{OC} - 4\overrightarrow{OI})\)
\(= 2(\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OA} - \overrightarrow{OI})) - (\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OI})) + 3(\overrightarrow{OI} + k(\overrightarrow{OC} - \overrightarrow{OI}))\)
\(= 2 \overrightarrow{OA'} - \overrightarrow{OB'} + 3 \overrightarrow{OC'}\)

Thus, \(4 \overrightarrow{OG'} = 2 \overrightarrow{OA'} - \overrightarrow{OB'} + 3 \overrightarrow{OC'}\), which means G' is the barycenter of (A',2), (B',-1), and (C',3).

Conclusion: Homothety preserves barycenters with the same weights.

V. Trapezoid Configuration

Activity 5: Trapezoid Analysis

In the opposite figure, ABCD is a trapezoid with bases [AB] and [CD]. I is the midpoint of [AB] and J is the midpoint of [CD]. O is the intersection point of lines (AD) and (BC), and O' is the intersection point of lines (AC) and (BD).

Figure: Trapezoid ABCD with intersection points O and O'
Trapezoid ABCD with midpoints I, J and intersection points O, O'.

1) Let h1 be the homothety with center O that transforms A into D.

a) Show that h1 transforms B into C.

b) Deduce that points O, I, and J are aligned.

1) a) Proof that h1 transforms B into C:

Since h1 is a homothety with center O and h1(A) = D, we have:

\(\overrightarrow{OD} = k_1 \overrightarrow{OA}\) for some ratio k‚ÇÅ

In trapezoid ABCD, (AB) ‚à• (CD). Consider lines (AD) and (BC) which intersect at O.

Triangles OAB and ODC are similar (by Thales theorem since (AB) ‚à• (CD)).

The homothety with center O that sends A to D must send B to C to preserve parallelism:

\(h_1(B) = C\)

b) Deduction that O, I, and J are aligned:

Since h1 is a homothety with center O, and I is the midpoint of [AB], the image of I under h1 is the midpoint of [h1(A)h1(B)] = [DC].

But the midpoint of [DC] is J. Therefore, h1(I) = J.

Since h1(I) = J and h1 has center O, points O, I, and J are aligned (a point, its image, and the center of homothety are always aligned).

2) Let h2 be the homothety with center O' that transforms A into C.

a) Show that h2 transforms B into D.

b) Show that points O, I, O', and J are aligned.

2) a) Proof that h2 transforms B into D:

Since h2 is a homothety with center O' and h2(A) = C, we have:

\(\overrightarrow{O'C} = k_2 \overrightarrow{O'A}\) for some ratio k‚ÇÇ

Consider triangles O'AB and O'CD. Lines (AC) and (BD) intersect at O'.

Since (AB) ‚à• (CD), by Thales theorem, triangles O'AB and O'DC are similar.

The homothety with center O' that sends A to C must send B to D to preserve parallelism:

\(h_2(B) = D\)

b) Proof that O, I, O', and J are aligned:

From part 1, we know O, I, and J are aligned.

Now consider h2. Since I is the midpoint of [AB] and h2 is a homothety, h2(I) is the midpoint of [h2(A)h2(B)] = [CD].

But the midpoint of [CD] is J. Therefore, h2(I) = J.

Since h2(I) = J and h2 has center O', points O', I, and J are aligned.

Thus, I and J are aligned with both O and O'. Therefore, all four points O, I, O', and J are aligned.

3) In the opposite figure, lines D1, D2, and D3 are parallel.

Show that points P, Q, and R are aligned.

3) Proof that P, Q, and R are aligned:

Let D1, D2, and D3 be three parallel lines.

Consider two transversals that intersect these lines at points A, B, C on D1; A', B', C' on D2; and A", B", C" on D3.

Let P = (AA') ‚à© (BB'), Q = (BB') ‚à© (CC'), and R = (AA') ‚à© (CC').

By Thales theorem on parallel lines D1, D2, D3:

\(\frac{AB}{BC} = \frac{A'B'}{B'C'} = \frac{A"B"}{B"C"}\)

Consider homothety h1 with center P that sends A to A'. Since (AB) ‚à• (A'B'), h1 must send B to B'.

Similarly, consider homothety h2 with center Q that sends B to B'. Since (BC) ‚à• (B'C'), h2 must send C to C'.

The composition h2 ‚àò h1 sends A to A' then to A", and sends C to C' then to C".

But h2 ‚àò h1 is also a homothety (composition of homotheties with aligned centers is a homothety).

This homothety sends A to A" and C to C", so its center must be the intersection of (AA") and (CC"), which is R.

Since P, Q, and R are centers of homotheties that are aligned (by the property of composition), points P, Q, and R are aligned.

This is a special case of the Pappus theorem for parallel lines.

Comprehensive Summary

Key Properties of Homothety

Through these exercises, we have demonstrated important properties of homotheties:

Properties Demonstrated

  • Conservation of alignment: The image of aligned points under a homothety remains aligned.
  • Transformation of lines: A homothety transforms a line into a parallel line.
  • Transformation of segments: The image of segment [AB] is segment [A'B'].
  • Conservation of midpoints: The image of the midpoint of a segment is the midpoint of the image segment.
  • Conservation of barycenters: Homotheties preserve barycenters with the same weights.
  • Geometric configurations: Homotheties are powerful tools for analyzing trapezoids and parallel line configurations.

Homothety is not just a simple scaling transformation; it's a powerful geometric tool that preserves many important properties. Mastering homotheties enables you to solve complex geometric problems, prove collinearity and concurrency, and understand deeper relationships in geometric configurations. These exercises demonstrate how homothety connects with other geometric concepts like parallelism, midpoints, barycenters, and similarity.
