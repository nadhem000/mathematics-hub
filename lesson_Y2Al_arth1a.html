Arithmetic: Part 1

Euclidean Division - Divisibility

Introduction

Euclidean division and divisibility are fundamental concepts in arithmetic that form the basis for many mathematical operations and proofs. These concepts are essential for understanding number theory and advanced mathematics.

In this lesson, we will explore the properties of divisibility, learn how to perform Euclidean division, and apply these concepts to solve various mathematical problems.

Activity 1: Understanding Divisibility

Let a be a natural integer and n a divisor of a. Indicate which of the following statements are true:

a) a is a multiple of n

TRUE. By definition, if n divides a, then there exists an integer k such that \(a = k \times n\), which means a is a multiple of n.

b) a and n are coprime (relatively prime)

FALSE. This is not necessarily true. For example, if \(a = 6\) and \(n = 2\), then n divides a but a and n are not coprime since \(\text{GCD}(6, 2) = 2 \neq 1\).

c) \(\text{GCD}(a, n) = n\)

TRUE. Since n divides a, the greatest common divisor of a and n is n itself.

d) \(a = k \times n\), where k is a natural integer

TRUE. This is the definition of divisibility: n divides a if and only if there exists an integer k such that \(a = k \times n\).

e) \(\text{LCM}(a, n) = n \times a\)

FALSE. The least common multiple of a and n is actually a itself, since a is a multiple of n. \(\text{LCM}(a, n) = a\) when n divides a.

f) The remainder of the Euclidean division of a by n is zero

TRUE. If n divides a, then \(a = k \times n\) with k integer, so when we divide a by n, the quotient is k and the remainder is 0.

Part 1: Euclidean Division - Divisibility

Activity 2: Properties of Divisibility

Let n, x, and y be three natural integers such that n divides x and n divides y.

a) Show that n divides \(p \times x\) and n divides \(p \times y\), where p is a natural integer.

Since n divides x, there exists an integer k such that \(x = k \times n\).

\(p \times x = p \times (k \times n) = (p \times k) \times n\)

Since \(p \times k\) is an integer, n divides \(p \times x\). The same reasoning applies for y.

b) Show that n divides \(x + y\).

Since n divides x and n divides y, there exist integers \(k_1\) and \(k_2\) such that:

\(x = k_1 \times n\) and \(y = k_2 \times n\)
\(x + y = k_1 \times n + k_2 \times n = (k_1 + k_2) \times n\)

Since \(k_1 + k_2\) is an integer, n divides \(x + y\).

c) Show that n divides \(x - y\) (assuming \(x \geq y\)).

Since n divides x and n divides y, there exist integers \(k_1\) and \(k_2\) such that:

\(x = k_1 \times n\) and \(y = k_2 \times n\)
\(x - y = k_1 \times n - k_2 \times n = (k_1 - k_2) \times n\)

Since \(k_1 - k_2\) is an integer (and \(x \geq y\) ensures it's non-negative), n divides \(x - y\).

Activity 3: Advanced Divisibility Problems

1. Show that if a natural integer divides 328 and divides 299, then it divides 29.

Let d be a natural integer that divides both 328 and 299. Then d divides any linear combination of 328 and 299.

\(328 - 299 = 29\)

Since d divides 328 and 299, by Activity 2(c), d also divides their difference \(328 - 299 = 29\).

2. Deduce that 328 and 299 are coprime.

The only positive divisors of 29 are 1 and 29. If a number d divides both 328 and 299, then it divides 29.

Check if 29 divides 328: \(328 \div 29 = 11\) remainder 9, so 29 does not divide 328.

Therefore, the only common divisor is 1, so \(\text{GCD}(328, 299) = 1\), meaning they are coprime.

3. Decompose 343 into prime factors and show that 118 and 225 are coprime.

Prime factorization of 343:

\(343 = 7 \times 49 = 7 \times 7 \times 7 = 7^3\)

Prime factorization of 118 and 225:

\(118 = 2 \times 59\)
\(225 = 3^2 \times 5^2\)

Since 118 and 225 have no common prime factors, they are coprime: \(\text{GCD}(118, 225) = 1\).

4. Let a, b, and n be three natural integers. Show that if n divides \(2a + 3b\) and n divides \(3a + 2b\), then n divides \(5a\) and n divides \(5b\).

Given: n divides \((2a + 3b)\) and n divides \((3a + 2b)\)

\(3 \times (2a + 3b) - 2 \times (3a + 2b) = 6a + 9b - 6a - 4b = 5b\)

Since n divides both \((2a + 3b)\) and \((3a + 2b)\), n divides any linear combination: n divides \(5b\).

\(2 \times (3a + 2b) - 3 \times (2a + 3b) = 6a + 4b - 6a - 9b = -5a\)

Thus n divides \(5a\) (since if n divides \(-5a\), it also divides \(5a\)).

5. Determine all natural integers n such that n divides \(n + 15\).

If n divides \((n + 15)\), then there exists an integer k such that:

\(n + 15 = k \times n\)
\(15 = k \times n - n = n \times (k - 1)\)

So n must be a divisor of 15. The divisors of 15 are: 1, 3, 5, 15.

Summary: Euclidean Division

Definition

Let a and b be two natural integers with \(b \neq 0\).

There exists a unique pair \((q, r)\) of natural integers such that:

\(a = b \times q + r\) with \(0 \leq r < b\)

Performing the Euclidean division of a by b means finding the pair \((q, r)\) of natural integers such that: \(a = b \times q + r\) with \(0 \leq r < b\).

  • a is the dividend
  • b is the divisor
  • q is the quotient
  • r is the remainder

Key Properties

  • If n divides a and n divides b, then n divides \((a + b)\) and \((a - b)\)
  • If n divides a, then n divides \((k \times a)\) for any integer k
  • If n divides a and n divides b, then n divides any linear combination \((m \times a + p \times b)\)
  • A number is divisible by another if and only if the remainder of their Euclidean division is zero

Exercise 2

Show that if a number divides both 84 and 108, then it also divides 24.

Let d be a divisor of both 84 and 108. Then:

\(108 - 84 = 24\)

Since d divides 108 and 84, by the properties of divisibility, d divides their difference 24.

If n divides \((2n + 8)\), then there exists integer k such that:

So n must be a divisor of 8: \(\{1, 2, 4, 8\}\)

Exercise 4

Prove that the product of three consecutive integers is divisible by 6.

Let the three consecutive integers be n, \(n+1\), and \(n+2\).

Among any three consecutive integers:

  • At least one is divisible by 2 (even)
  • Exactly one is divisible by 3
