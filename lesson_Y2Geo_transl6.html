Exercise 1
In the adjacent figure, C and C' are two circles intersecting at A and B. Construct a point M on C and a point M' on C' such that A is the midpoint of [MM'].
Figure: Two circles C and C' intersecting at A and B
Circles C and C' intersect at A and B. Construct M on C and M' on C' such that A is midpoint of MM'.
Hint
Use the image of C by the half-turn (central symmetry) about point A.

Solution

Let \(S_A\) be the half-turn (central symmetry) about point A. We know that \(S_A\) is an isometry that maps any point P to P' such that A is the midpoint of [PP'].

If we take M on circle C, then M' = \(S_A(M)\) is such that A is the midpoint of [MM']. We need M' to lie on C'.

Therefore, M' must belong to both \(S_A(C)\) and C'. So M' is an intersection point of \(S_A(C)\) and C'.

Construction steps:

  1. Construct the image of circle C under \(S_A\) (half-turn about A)
  2. Find the intersection points of \(S_A(C)\) and C' (besides A)
  3. Let M' be one of these intersection points
  4. Construct M = \(S_A(M')\) (which lies on C)

Since A is the center of symmetry, it is the midpoint of [MM'] by construction.

Exercise 2
A bridge problem: In the adjacent figure, lines D and D' represent the (parallel) banks of a river. Where should the bridge [MN] (perpendicular to the river banks) be constructed so that the path from village A to village B is as short as possible?
Figure: River with parallel banks D and D', villages A and B
Lines D and D' are parallel river banks. A and B are villages. Find optimal bridge MN perpendicular to banks.
Hint
Notice that:
  • \(\overrightarrow{MN}\) is a constant vector (perpendicular to the banks, fixed length equal to river width)
  • AM = A'N, where A' is the image of A by the translation of vector \(\overrightarrow{MN}\)

Solution

Let w be the width of the river (distance between D and D'). The bridge MN is perpendicular to the banks, so \(MN = w\) and \(\overrightarrow{MN}\) is constant (same direction and magnitude for any position of the bridge).

Let t be the translation of vector \(\overrightarrow{MN}\). Then A' = t(A) is the image of A under this translation.

The total path from A to B via the bridge is: \(AM + MN + NB\)

Since MN = w is constant, minimizing the total path is equivalent to minimizing \(AM + NB\).

But note that \(AM = A'N\) (since A' = t(A) and N = t(M), so \(\overrightarrow{A'N} = \overrightarrow{AM}\)).

Therefore, \(AM + NB = A'N + NB\).

The shortest path from A' to B is a straight line. So we should choose N such that A', N, and B are collinear.

Construction steps:

  1. Translate point A by the vector perpendicular to D and D' with magnitude w (the river width) to get A'
  2. Draw line A'B
  3. Let N be the intersection of A'B with the far bank D'
  4. Construct M by translating N back by the opposite vector (so MN is perpendicular to banks)

This gives the optimal position for the bridge.

Exercise 3
Let D be a line, A a point in the plane, and l a positive real number. Construct a circle passing through A that intersects line D at two points which are endpoints of a segment of length l.
Figure: Line D, point A, and circle through A intersecting D in segment of length l
Construct circle through A that cuts D in a chord of length l.
Hint
  • Start by constructing a circle C that cuts D in a segment of length l (temporarily ignoring the condition "C passes through A").
  • Find a translation that transforms C into a circle passing through A and still cutting D in a segment of length l.

Solution

Let C be a circle that cuts D in a segment of length l. The center O of such a circle must lie on a line parallel to D at a distance \(d = \sqrt{R^2 - (l/2)^2}\) from D, where R is the radius of C.

We can construct one such circle Câ‚€ with center Oâ‚€ on the perpendicular bisector of any segment of length l on D.

Now we need a circle C that passes through A and cuts D in a segment of length l. Let t be a translation that sends Câ‚€ to C. Since translations preserve distances, if Câ‚€ cuts D in a segment of length l, then C = t(Câ‚€) also cuts D in a segment of length l.

We need to find a translation t such that A âˆˆ t(Câ‚€). This means A is the image of some point on Câ‚€ under t.

Construction steps:

  1. Construct any circle Câ‚€ that cuts D in a segment of length l
  2. Choose any point P on Câ‚€
  3. Let t be the translation that sends P to A: \(\overrightarrow{PA}\)
  4. Then C = t(Câ‚€) is a circle passing through A (since t(P) = A) and cutting D in a segment of length l

Note: There are two possible circles (depending on which side of D we place the center).

Exercise 4
Let ABCD be a parallelogram and E a point not on line (BC). The parallel to line (BE) through A and the parallel to line (CE) through D intersect at point F. Show that EF = BA.
Figure: Parallelogram ABCD with point E, lines through A and D parallel to BE and CE respectively
Parallelogram ABCD. Point E not on BC. Lines: through A parallel to BE, through D parallel to CE. They intersect at F.

Solution

Consider the translation t that sends B to A. Since ABCD is a parallelogram, we have \(\overrightarrow{BA} = \overrightarrow{CD}\).

Under translation t: B â†’ A, and since t preserves directions, line (BE) is mapped to a line through A parallel to (BE), which is exactly the line through A in the problem statement.

Similarly, consider the translation t' that sends C to D. Since \(\overrightarrow{CD} = \overrightarrow{BA}\), t' has the same vector as t.

Under t': C â†’ D, and line (CE) is mapped to a line through D parallel to (CE), which is the other line in the problem.

Now, let F be the intersection of these two lines. Since both lines are images of lines through E under translations with the same vector \(\overrightarrow{BA}\), point F is the image of E under this translation.

Therefore, \(\overrightarrow{EF} = \overrightarrow{BA}\), which implies EF = BA and EF âˆ¥ BA.

Formally:
Line through A parallel to BE: t(BE) where t is translation by \(\overrightarrow{BA}\)
Line through D parallel to CE: t'(CE) where t' is translation by \(\overrightarrow{CD} = \overrightarrow{BA}\)
Since E is on both original lines, t(E) is on the first line and t'(E) is on the second line.
But t(E) = t'(E) because both translations have the same vector.
Thus F = t(E) = t'(E), so \(\overrightarrow{EF} = \overrightarrow{BA}\).

Exercise 5
Let C be a circle with center O and radius R, and let D be a line. For every point M on C, draw the circle C_M with center M and radius R, then draw the tangents to C_M that are parallel to D. What is the set described by the points of contact of these tangents with the circles C_M?
Figure: Circle C with center O, line D, and circles C_M centered on C with tangents parallel to D
For M on circle C, draw circle C_M with center M and radius R. Draw tangents to C_M parallel to D. Locus of contact points?

Solution

Let M be a point on circle C. Circle C_M has center M and radius R.

The tangents to C_M parallel to D come in two parallel lines (one on each side of C_M). Let T be one of the contact points.

Since the tangent at T is parallel to D, the radius MT is perpendicular to D.

Thus T lies on the line through M perpendicular to D. Also, MT = R (radius of C_M).

Now consider the translation t that sends O to M. Since M is on circle C with center O and radius R, we have OM = R.

Under translation t, point O goes to M. What happens to the contact point T?

Let Tâ‚€ be the point on the line through O perpendicular to D such that OTâ‚€ = R (there are two such points, one on each side of O).

Then T = t(Tâ‚€), because:
- t preserves distances: MT = OTâ‚€ = R
- t preserves directions: Since \(\overrightarrow{OM}\) is arbitrary (M moves on circle C), but we know MT is perpendicular to D, and OTâ‚€ is also perpendicular to D

Thus as M varies on circle C, T varies on the circle with center O and radius \(R\sqrt{2}\) (since OT = OM + MT in vector sense, but careful: OTâ‚€ has length R, and O to T is O to M plus M to T, which are not necessarily collinear).

Actually, more precisely: \(\overrightarrow{OT} = \overrightarrow{OM} + \overrightarrow{MT}\), where \(|\overrightarrow{OM}| = R\) and \(|\overrightarrow{MT}| = R\), and \(\overrightarrow{MT} \perp D\).

Since M moves on circle C (center O, radius R), and MT has constant length R and fixed direction (perpendicular to D), T moves on a circle with center O and radius \(R\sqrt{2}\) only if OM and MT are perpendicular. But they're not necessarily perpendicular.

The correct answer: The set of contact points is two lines parallel to D at distance R from O (one on each side of O). Let's prove this:

Let D' be the line through O perpendicular to D. Let Tâ‚€ be a point on D' such that OTâ‚€ = R. For any M on circle C, the contact point T satisfies \(\overrightarrow{MT} = \overrightarrow{OTâ‚€}\) (same length R and direction perpendicular to D).

So \(\overrightarrow{OT} = \overrightarrow{OM} + \overrightarrow{OTâ‚€}\). As M varies on circle C, \(\overrightarrow{OM}\) varies, but \(\overrightarrow{OTâ‚€}\) is fixed. Thus T varies on the circle with center Tâ‚€ and radius R.

Wait, that's not right either. Let's think differently: The condition is that MT âŸ‚ D and MT = R. So T is on the line through M perpendicular to D, at distance R from M.

This means T is obtained from M by a translation of fixed vector \(\vec{v}\) (perpendicular to D, length R). There are two possible vectors: \(\vec{v}\) and \(-\vec{v}\).

Thus T = M + \(\vec{v}\) or T = M - \(\vec{v}\), where M varies on circle C.

Therefore, the set of all such T is the translation of circle C by vector \(\vec{v}\) and by vector -\(\vec{v}\). These are two circles congruent to C, with centers O+\(\vec{v}\) and O-\(\vec{v}\).

So the locus is two circles.

Exercise 6
Let A and B be two points on a circle C. Construct two points M and N on C such that ABMN is a trapezoid with bases [AB] and [MN] and AB = 2MN.
Figure: Circle C with points A and B, trapezoid ABMN with bases AB and MN
Construct M and N on circle C such that ABMN is trapezoid with AB âˆ¥ MN and AB = 2MN.

Solution

Since ABMN is a trapezoid with bases [AB] and [MN], we have AB âˆ¥ MN.

The condition AB = 2MN means that MN = AB/2.

Consider the midpoint I of [AB]. Since A and B are on circle C, I is inside the circle (unless AB is a diameter, then I is the center).

Now, MN is parallel to AB and half its length. So if we let J be the midpoint of [MN], then IJ is perpendicular to AB (and to MN).

Also, M and N are symmetric with respect to the line through the center O perpendicular to AB (since they're on the circle and MN âˆ¥ AB).

Let d be the distance between lines AB and MN. Then by the Pythagorean theorem in the right triangle formed by the radius, half of MN, and distance from center to MN, we can find d.

Let R be the radius of circle C, and let h be the distance from O to line AB. Then:
\(R^2 = h^2 + (AB/2)^2\)
For line MN at distance d from AB (with MN on the opposite side of AB from O, or same side depending on configuration):
\(R^2 = (h Â± d)^2 + (MN/2)^2 = (h Â± d)^2 + (AB/4)^2\)

Subtracting: \(0 = (h Â± d)^2 - h^2 + (AB/4)^2 - (AB/2)^2\)
\((h Â± d)^2 - h^2 = (AB/2)^2 - (AB/4)^2 = (3/16)AB^2\)
\(d^2 Â± 2hd = (3/16)AB^2\)

This quadratic in d can be solved. Alternatively, we can use translations:

Let t be the translation that sends A to the midpoint of AB. Then t(AB) is a segment parallel to AB of length AB/2 starting at the midpoint of AB.

We need MN to be this translated segment, with M and N on circle C. So M and N are intersection points of circle C with the line parallel to AB at appropriate distance.

Construction steps:

  1. Find midpoint I of AB
  2. Construct line through I parallel to AB (this is the midline of the trapezoid)
  3. On this line, mark points J and J' such that IJ = IJ' = AB/4 (since MN = AB/2, its half is AB/4)
  4. Through J and J', draw lines parallel to AB (these are possible positions for MN)
  5. Find intersections of these lines with circle C to get M and N
  6. Check which pair gives ABMN as a trapezoid (with A, B, M, N in that order around the circle)

There are generally two solutions (one with MN on each side of AB).

Summary of Key Techniques

Translation Techniques Used in These Exercises

  • Using translations to construct points with specific midpoint properties (Exercise 1)
  • Applying translations to minimize path length (Exercise 2 - bridge problem)
  • Translating geometric figures to satisfy additional conditions (Exercise 3)
  • Using translations to prove length equalities in complex configurations (Exercise 4)
  • Analyzing loci using translations (Exercise 5)
  • Constructing figures with specific metric relationships using translations (Exercise 6)

Problem-Solving Strategies

  • Identify fixed vectors in the problem that suggest translations
  • Use translations to transform difficult conditions into simpler ones
  • Look for parallel lines and equal vectors as indicators of translations
  • When constructing, build a simpler figure first then translate it to meet conditions

Conclusion

These exercises demonstrate the power of translations in solving diverse geometric problems. From construction to proof to optimization, translations provide a powerful tool for simplifying complex geometric relationships. Mastering these techniques will greatly enhance your problem-solving abilities in geometry.
