Polynomials: Part 3

Factorisation of Polynomials

Introduction to Polynomial Factorisation

In this lesson, we'll explore the concept of polynomial roots and learn how to factor polynomials. Factorisation is a powerful technique that allows us to rewrite polynomials as products of simpler polynomials, which is essential for solving polynomial equations and understanding their properties.

Level Note

This lesson builds upon your knowledge of polynomial operations and equality. We're advancing to more complex algebraic concepts that will be fundamental for solving higher-degree equations.

Roots of Polynomials and Factorisation

Definition: Root of a Polynomial

A real number \( \alpha \) is called a root or zero of a polynomial \( f \) if \( f(\alpha) = 0 \).

Activity 11: Evaluating Polynomials

Let \( f(x) = x^2 + 2x - 3 \), \( g(x) = x^3 + 2x^2 - 1 \), and \( h(x) = 3x^3 - x^2 - 6x + 2 \).

Part A: Calculate \( f(-1) \), \( f(1) \), and \( f(-3) \)

Step 1: Calculate \( f(-1) \)

\( f(-1) = (-1)^2 + 2(-1) - 3 = 1 - 2 - 3 = -4 \)

Step 2: Calculate \( f(1) \)

\( f(1) = (1)^2 + 2(1) - 3 = 1 + 2 - 3 = 0 \)

Step 3: Calculate \( f(-3) \)

\( f(-3) = (-3)^2 + 2(-3) - 3 = 9 - 6 - 3 = 0 \)

Conclusion: \( f(1) = 0 \) and \( f(-3) = 0 \), so 1 and -3 are roots of \( f \).

Part B: Calculate \( g(-1) \), \( g(0) \), and \( g(\sqrt{2}) \)

Step 1: Calculate \( g(-1) \)

\( g(-1) = (-1)^3 + 2(-1)^2 - 1 = -1 + 2 - 1 = 0 \)

Step 2: Calculate \( g(0) \)

\( g(0) = (0)^3 + 2(0)^2 - 1 = -1 \)

Step 3: Calculate \( g(\sqrt{2}) \)

\( g(\sqrt{2}) = (\sqrt{2})^3 + 2(\sqrt{2})^2 - 1 = 2\sqrt{2} + 4 - 1 = 2\sqrt{2} + 3 \)

Conclusion: \( g(-1) = 0 \), so -1 is a root of \( g \).

Part C: Calculate \( h(0) \), \( h\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) \), and \( h(2) \)

Step 1: Calculate \( h(0) \)

\( h(0) = 3(0)^3 - (0)^2 - 6(0) + 2 = 2 \)

Step 2: Calculate \( h\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) \)

\( h\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) = 3\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^3 - \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 - 6\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) + 2 = 3\left(\frac{1}{27}\right) - \frac{1}{9} - 2 + 2 = \frac{1}{9} - \frac{1}{9} = 0 \)

Step 3: Calculate \( h(2) \)

\( h(2) = 3(2)^3 - (2)^2 - 6(2) + 2 = 24 - 4 - 12 + 2 = 10 \)

Conclusion: \( h\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) = 0 \), so \( \frac{1}{3} \) is a root of \( h \).

Activity 12: Verifying Roots

Show that the real number \( \alpha \) is a root of the given polynomial.

Part A: \( f(x) = 5x^2 + x - 6 \); \( \alpha = 1 \)

Step 1: Substitute \( \alpha = 1 \) into \( f(x) \)

\( f(1) = 5(1)^2 + 1 - 6 = 5 + 1 - 6 = 0 \)

Step 2: Conclusion

Since \( f(1) = 0 \), \( \alpha = 1 \) is indeed a root of \( f(x) \).

Part B: \( g(x) = 4x^4 + 5x^3 - 14x^2 - 9x + 14 \); \( \alpha = -2 \)

Step 1: Substitute \( \alpha = -2 \) into \( g(x) \)

\( g(-2) = 4(-2)^4 + 5(-2)^3 - 14(-2)^2 - 9(-2) + 14 \)
\( = 4(16) + 5(-8) - 14(4) + 18 + 14 \)
\( = 64 - 40 - 56 + 18 + 14 = 0 \)

Step 2: Conclusion

Since \( g(-2) = 0 \), \( \alpha = -2 \) is indeed a root of \( g(x) \).

Part C: \( h(x) = 8x^5 + 3x^2 - 1 \); \( \alpha = \frac{1}{2} \)

Step 1: Substitute \( \alpha = \frac{1}{2} \) into \( h(x) \)

\( h\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 8\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^5 + 3\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 - 1 \)
\( = 8\left(\frac{1}{32}\right) + 3\left(\frac{1}{4}\right) - 1 \)
\( = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{4} - 1 = 1 - 1 = 0 \)

Step 2: Conclusion

Since \( h\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 0 \), \( \alpha = \frac{1}{2} \) is indeed a root of \( h(x) \).

Activity 13: Finding Coefficients Using Roots

Let P be the polynomial defined by \(P(x) = ax^4 + (a + 1)x^3 - (b+9)x^2 - (2b-1)x + 14\), where a and b are real numbers.

Part A: Determine a and b knowing that 1 and -2 are two roots of the polynomial P

Step 1: Use the fact that 1 is a root of P

\(P(1) = a(1)^4 + (a + 1)(1)^3 - (b+9)(1)^2 - (2b-1)(1) + 14 = 0\)
\(a + (a + 1) - (b+9) - (2b-1) + 14 = 0\)
\(2a + 1 - b - 9 - 2b + 1 + 14 = 0\)
\(2a - 3b + 7 = 0\)

Step 2: Use the fact that -2 is a root of P

\(P(-2) = a(-2)^4 + (a + 1)(-2)^3 - (b+9)(-2)^2 - (2b-1)(-2) + 14 = 0\)
\(16a + (a + 1)(-8) - (b+9)(4) + 2(2b-1) + 14 = 0\)
\(16a - 8a - 8 - 4b - 36 + 4b - 2 + 14 = 0\)
\(8a - 32 = 0\)
\(8a = 32\)
\(a = 4\)

Step 3: Substitute a = 4 into the first equation

\(2(4) - 3b + 7 = 0\)
\(8 - 3b + 7 = 0\)
\(15 - 3b = 0\)
\(3b = 15\)
\(b = 5\)

Conclusion: a = 4 and b = 5

Part B: With a and b found, determine the polynomial Q such that \(P(x) = (x^2 + x - 2)Q(x)\) for all real x

Step 1: Write P(x) with the values a = 4 and b = 5

\(P(x) = 4x^4 + (4 + 1)x^3 - (5+9)x^2 - (2(5)-1)x + 14\)
\(P(x) = 4x^4 + 5x^3 - 14x^2 - 9x + 14\)

Step 2: Factor \(x^2 + x - 2\)

\(x^2 + x - 2 = (x - 1)(x + 2)\)

Step 3: Perform polynomial division of P(x) by \(x^2 + x - 2\)

\(4x^4 + 5x^3 - 14x^2 - 9x + 14 \div (x^2 + x - 2)\)
First term: \(4x^2\)
Multiply: \(4x^2(x^2 + x - 2) = 4x^4 + 4x^3 - 8x^2\)
Subtract: \((4x^4 + 5x^3 - 14x^2) - (4x^4 + 4x^3 - 8x^2) = x^3 - 6x^2\)
Bring down next term: \(x^3 - 6x^2 - 9x\)
Next term: \(x\)
Multiply: \(x(x^2 + x - 2) = x^3 + x^2 - 2x\)
Subtract: \((x^3 - 6x^2 - 9x) - (x^3 + x^2 - 2x) = -7x^2 - 7x\)
Bring down next term: \(-7x^2 - 7x + 14\)
Next term: \(-7\)
Multiply: \(-7(x^2 + x - 2) = -7x^2 - 7x + 14\)
Subtract: \((-7x^2 - 7x + 14) - (-7x^2 - 7x + 14) = 0\)

Conclusion: \(Q(x) = 4x^2 + x - 7\)

Activity 14: Factorizing Polynomials

Factorize each of the polynomials A, B, C and D defined by:

Part A: Factorize \(A(x) = x^4 - 3x^3 + 2x^2\)

Step 1: Factor out the common factor \(x^2\)

\(A(x) = x^2(x^2 - 3x + 2)\)

Step 2: Factor the quadratic \(x^2 - 3x + 2\)

\(x^2 - 3x + 2 = (x - 1)(x - 2)\)

Conclusion: \(A(x) = x^2(x - 1)(x - 2)\)

Part B: Factorize \(B(x) = (x^2 + 5x) + 2(x+5)^3\)

Step 1: Factor out x from the first term

\(B(x) = x(x + 5) + 2(x+5)^3\)

Step 2: Factor out the common factor \((x + 5)\)

\(B(x) = (x + 5)[x + 2(x+5)^2]\)

Step 3: Expand and simplify the expression inside the brackets

\(x + 2(x+5)^2 = x + 2(x^2 + 10x + 25)\)
\(= x + 2x^2 + 20x + 50\)
\(= 2x^2 + 21x + 50\)

Step 4: Factor the quadratic \(2x^2 + 21x + 50\)

\(2x^2 + 21x + 50 = (2x + 5)(x + 10)\)

Conclusion: \(B(x) = (x + 5)(2x + 5)(x + 10)\)

Part C: Factorize \(C(x) = 2x^3 + 4x^2 + 3x + 6\)

Step 1: Group terms to find common factors

\(C(x) = (2x^3 + 4x^2) + (3x + 6)\)

Step 2: Factor out common factors from each group

\(C(x) = 2x^2(x + 2) + 3(x + 2)\)

Step 3: Factor out the common factor \((x + 2)\)

\(C(x) = (x + 2)(2x^2 + 3)\)

Conclusion: \(C(x) = (x + 2)(2x^2 + 3)\)

Part D: Factorize \(D(x) = x^4 + 3x^3 - x - 3\)

Step 1: Group terms to find common factors

\(D(x) = (x^4 + 3x^3) + (-x - 3)\)

Step 2: Factor out common factors from each group

\(D(x) = x^3(x + 3) - 1(x + 3)\)

Step 3: Factor out the common factor \((x + 3)\)

\(D(x) = (x + 3)(x^3 - 1)\)

Step 4: Factor \(x^3 - 1\) as a difference of cubes

\(x^3 - 1 = (x - 1)(x^2 + x + 1)\)

Conclusion: \(D(x) = (x + 3)(x - 1)(x^2 + x + 1)\)

Activity 15: Working with Polynomials P and Q

Let P and Q be the polynomials defined by \(P(x) = 3x^3 + x^2 + 2x - 6\) and \(Q(x) = x^3 + 3x^2 - 4x - 12\).

Part 1a: Verify that 1 is a root of P

Step 1: Substitute x = 1 into P(x)

\(P(1) = 3(1)^3 + (1)^2 + 2(1) - 6\)
\(= 3 + 1 + 2 - 6\)
\(= 0\)

Conclusion: Since P(1) = 0, 1 is indeed a root of P

Part 1b: Determine three real numbers a, b and c such that for all real x, \(P(x) = (x - 1)(ax^2 + bx + c)\)

Step 1: Perform polynomial division of P(x) by (x - 1)

\(3x^3 + x^2 + 2x - 6 \div (x - 1)\)
First term: \(3x^2\)
Multiply: \(3x^2(x - 1) = 3x^3 - 3x^2\)
Subtract: \((3x^3 + x^2) - (3x^3 - 3x^2) = 4x^2\)
Bring down next term: \(4x^2 + 2x\)
Next term: \(4x\)
Multiply: \(4x(x - 1) = 4x^2 - 4x\)
Subtract: \((4x^2 + 2x) - (4x^2 - 4x) = 6x\)
Bring down next term: \(6x - 6\)
Next term: \(6\)
Multiply: \(6(x - 1) = 6x - 6\)
Subtract: \((6x - 6) - (6x - 6) = 0\)

Conclusion: \(a = 3\), \(b = 4\), \(c = 6\), so \(P(x) = (x - 1)(3x^2 + 4x + 6)\)

Part 2a: Calculate Q(2) and Q(-3)

Step 1: Calculate Q(2)

\(Q(2) = (2)^3 + 3(2)^2 - 4(2) - 12\)
\(= 8 + 12 - 8 - 12\)
\(= 0\)

Step 2: Calculate Q(-3)

\(Q(-3) = (-3)^3 + 3(-3)^2 - 4(-3) - 12\)
\(= -27 + 27 + 12 - 12\)
\(= 0\)

Conclusion: Q(2) = 0 and Q(-3) = 0, so 2 and -3 are roots of Q

Part 2b: Determine the polynomial R such that for all real x, \(Q(x) = (x - 2)(x + 3)R(x)\)

Step 1: First, multiply (x - 2)(x + 3)

\((x - 2)(x + 3) = x^2 + 3x - 2x - 6 = x^2 + x - 6\)

Step 2: Perform polynomial division of Q(x) by \(x^2 + x - 6\)

\(x^3 + 3x^2 - 4x - 12 \div (x^2 + x - 6)\)
First term: \(x\)
Multiply: \(x(x^2 + x - 6) = x^3 + x^2 - 6x\)
Subtract: \((x^3 + 3x^2 - 4x) - (x^3 + x^2 - 6x) = 2x^2 + 2x\)
Bring down next term: \(2x^2 + 2x - 12\)
Next term: \(2\)
Multiply: \(2(x^2 + x - 6) = 2x^2 + 2x - 12\)
Subtract: \((2x^2 + 2x - 12) - (2x^2 + 2x - 12) = 0\)

Conclusion: \(R(x) = x + 2\), so \(Q(x) = (x - 2)(x + 3)(x + 2)\)

Summary

Key Concepts

  • A root of a polynomial is a value that makes the polynomial equal to zero
  • If \( \alpha \) is a root of polynomial \( P(x) \), then \( (x - \alpha) \) is a factor of \( P(x) \)
  • A polynomial of degree \( n \) has at most \( n \) real roots
  • Factorisation allows us to rewrite polynomials as products of simpler polynomials
  • Finding roots is essential for solving polynomial equations

Understanding polynomial roots and factorisation is crucial for solving polynomial equations and analyzing polynomial functions. These concepts form the foundation for more advanced algebraic techniques you'll encounter in your mathematical journey.
