Arithmetic: Part 4

Divisibility Criteria by 11

Introduction

In this lesson, we explore the divisibility criterion by 11, which is based on the alternating sum of digits. This criterion is unique because it considers the positions of digits and uses alternating signs.

The divisibility by 11 rule is particularly useful for checking divisibility of large numbers and has interesting mathematical properties related to the decimal representation of numbers.

Part c: Observation

What do you notice?

We notice that \(d + m\) is equal to the remainder of \(n\) divided by 11 (except when \(d + m = 11\), in which case the remainder is 0). This shows that the alternating sum of digits (with appropriate adjustment when negative) gives us the remainder modulo 11.

Summary: Divisibility by 11

Divisibility by 11

Let \(n\) be a natural integer. Let \(S_1\) be the sum of its digits in odd positions (from right to left, starting with position 1 for the units digit) and \(S_2\) be the sum of its digits in even positions. Let \(d = S_1 - S_2\).

Case 1: \(d \geq 0\)

â€¢ \(n\) is divisible by 11 if and only if \(d\) is divisible by 11.

â€¢ The remainder of the Euclidean division of \(n\) by 11 is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division of \(d\) by 11.

Case 2: \(d < 0\)

Let \(p\) be the smallest natural integer such that \(d + 11p\) is positive or zero.

â€¢ \(n\) is divisible by 11 if and only if \(d + 11p\) is divisible by 11.

â€¢ The remainder of the Euclidean division of \(n\) by 11 is \(d + 11p\).

Example: 121 is divisible by 11 because \(1 - 2 + 1 = 0\) is divisible by 11.

Example: 253 is divisible by 11 because \(3 - 5 + 2 = 0\) is divisible by 11.

Example: 308 has \(d = 8 - 0 + 3 = 11\), which is divisible by 11, so 308 is divisible by 11.

Exercise 2: Remainder Calculation

Find the remainder when 123456 is divided by 11.

Exercise 3: Finding Missing Digits

Find all possible values of the digit \(x\) such that the number \(73x45\) is divisible by 11.

For the number \(73x45\), the alternating sum of digits is:

\(5 - 4 + x - 3 + 7 = x + 5\)

For divisibility by 11, \(x + 5\) must be divisible by 11. Since \(x\) is a digit (0-9), \(x + 5\) can be 0 or 11.

If \(x + 5 = 0\), then \(x = -5\) (not a valid digit).

If \(x + 5 = 11\), then \(x = 6\).

Therefore, the only possible value is \(x = 6\).

Exercise 4: Mathematical Proof

Prove the divisibility criterion by 11 using the fact that \(10 \equiv -1 \pmod{11}\).

Let \(n\) be a number with digits \(a_k a_{k-1} ... a_1 a_0\).

\(n = a_k \times 10^k + a_{k-1} \times 10^{k-1} + ... + a_1 \times 10 + a_0\)

Since \(10 \equiv -1 \pmod{11}\), we have \(10^m \equiv (-1)^m \pmod{11}\).

Therefore:

\(n \equiv a_k \times (-1)^k + a_{k-1} \times (-1)^{k-1} + ... + a_1 \times (-1) + a_0 \pmod{11}\)

This is equivalent to:

\(n \equiv a_0 - a_1 + a_2 - a_3 + ... + (-1)^k a_k \pmod{11}\)

Which is exactly the alternating sum of digits starting from the units digit.

Thus, \(n\) is divisible by 11 if and only if this alternating sum is divisible by 11.

Conclusion

Key Concepts Mastered

  • Divisibility criterion for 11 based on alternating sum of digits
  • Finding remainders using alternating digit sums
  • Handling negative alternating sums by adding multiples of 11
  • Mathematical justification using modular arithmetic
  • Application to problem-solving with divisibility by 11

The divisibility criterion by 11 is a powerful tool that demonstrates the elegant connection between a number's decimal representation and its divisibility properties. Unlike criteria for 2, 5, 4, 25, and 8 which depend on the last few digits, or criteria for 3 and 9 which use the sum of all digits, the criterion for 11 uses an alternating sum, making it unique among divisibility rules.
