Arithmetic: Part 2

Divisibility Criteria by 2, 5, 4, 25, and 8

Introduction

Divisibility criteria are simple rules that allow us to quickly determine whether one number is divisible by another without performing the actual division. These criteria are particularly useful for numbers like 2, 5, 4, 25, and 8, which have patterns based on the last digits of a number.

In this lesson, we will explore the mathematical foundations of these divisibility criteria and learn how to apply them to solve various problems efficiently.

Apply Exercises: Understanding Patterns

Instructions

Activity 1: Understanding the Pattern

Part a: Remainders by 1000

For each of the numbers 23, 96, 249, 1672, 3502, and 19456, determine the remainder of its Euclidean division by 1000. What do you notice?

For each number, the remainder when divided by 1000 is simply the last three digits:

\(23 \div 1000 = 0\) remainder \(23\)
\(96 \div 1000 = 0\) remainder \(96\)
\(249 \div 1000 = 0\) remainder \(249\)
\(1672 \div 1000 = 1\) remainder \(672\)
\(3502 \div 1000 = 3\) remainder \(502\)
\(19456 \div 1000 = 19\) remainder \(456\)

Observation: The remainder is exactly the number formed by the last three digits of the original number.

Part b: Remainders by 8

For each of the previous numbers, determine the remainder of its Euclidean division by 8.

\(23 \div 8 = 2\) remainder \(7\)
\(96 \div 8 = 12\) remainder \(0\)
\(249 \div 8 = 31\) remainder \(1\)
\(1672 \div 8 = 209\) remainder \(0\)
\(3502 \div 8 = 437\) remainder \(6\)
\(19456 \div 8 = 2432\) remainder \(0\)

Activity 2: Mathematical Justification

Part a: Number Representation

Let \(n\) be a natural number greater than or equal to 100 and let \(b\) be the number formed by its last three digits. Justify writing \(n\) in the form \(n = 1000a + b\), where \(a\) is a natural number.

Any natural number \(n \geq 100\) can be written in the decimal system as:

\(n = a_k \times 10^k + a_{k-1} \times 10^{k-1} + \ldots + a_3 \times 10^3 + a_2 \times 10^2 + a_1 \times 10 + a_0\)

We can group the terms:

\(n = (a_k \times 10^{k-3} + a_{k-1} \times 10^{k-4} + \ldots + a_3) \times 1000 + (a_2 \times 100 + a_1 \times 10 + a_0)\)

Let \(a = a_k \times 10^{k-3} + a_{k-1} \times 10^{k-4} + \ldots + a_3\) (which is a natural number) and \(b = a_2 \times 100 + a_1 \times 10 + a_0\) (the number formed by the last three digits). Therefore:

\(n = 1000a + b\)

Part b: Remainder Equality

Show that \(n\) and \(b\) have the same remainder in the Euclidean division by 8.

We have \(n = 1000a + b\). Now \(1000 = 8 \times 125\), so \(1000\) is divisible by 8.

When we divide \(n\) by 8:

\(n \div 8 = (1000a + b) \div 8 = (1000a) \div 8 + b \div 8\)

Since \(1000a\) is divisible by 8, the remainder when dividing \(n\) by 8 depends only on \(b\). Therefore, \(n\) and \(b\) have the same remainder when divided by 8.

Part c: Divisibility Criterion

State a divisibility criterion by 8.

Divisibility Criterion by 8

A natural number \(n\) (\(n \geq 100\)) is divisible by 8 if and only if the number formed by its last three digits is divisible by 8.

The remainder of the Euclidean division of \(n\) by 8 is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division by 8 of the number formed by its last three digits.

Activity 3: Application to Powers

Show that \(2^{721} + 2^{723}\) is divisible by 5.

First, factor the expression:

\(2^{721} + 2^{723} = 2^{721}(1 + 2^2) = 2^{721}(1 + 4) = 2^{721} \times 5\)

Therefore, the expression is clearly divisible by 5 since it contains a factor of 5.

Exercise 2: Remainder of Powers

Determine the remainder when \(3^{2024}\) is divided by 5.

Look at the pattern of units digits for powers of 3:

\(3^1 = 3\) â†’ units digit: \(3\)
\(3^2 = 9\) â†’ units digit: \(9\)
\(3^3 = 27\) â†’ units digit: \(7\)
\(3^4 = 81\) â†’ units digit: \(1\)
\(3^5 = 243\) â†’ units digit: \(3\) (pattern repeats every 4)

Since \(2024 \div 4 = 506\) exactly, \(3^{2024}\) ends with the same digit as \(3^4\), which is 1.

Therefore, \(3^{2024} \div 5\) gives a remainder equal to the remainder of \(1 \div 5\), which is 1.

Exercise 3: Product Consecutive Integers

Show that the product of any three consecutive integers is divisible by 8.

Let the three consecutive integers be \(n\), \(n+1\), and \(n+2\).

Among any three consecutive integers, at least one is even (divisible by 2).

Also, among any three consecutive integers, at least one is divisible by 4 (since every other even number is divisible by 4).

Therefore, the product contains factors 2 and 4, so it contains factor \(2 \times 4 = 8\).

Thus, the product of any three consecutive integers is divisible by 8.

Exercise 4: Smallest Power

Find the smallest natural number \(n\) such that \(2^n - 1\) is divisible by 5.

We need to find when \(2^n \equiv 1 \pmod{5}\).

Check powers of 2 modulo 5:

\(2^1 = 2 \equiv 2 \pmod{5}\)
\(2^2 = 4 \equiv 4 \pmod{5}\)
\(2^3 = 8 \equiv 3 \pmod{5}\)
\(2^4 = 16 \equiv 1 \pmod{5}\)

So \(2^4 - 1\) is divisible by 5. Check smaller values: \(2^1 - 1 = 1\), \(2^2 - 1 = 3\), \(2^3 - 1 = 7\), none divisible by 5.

Therefore, the smallest natural number \(n\) is 4.
