Homothety: Part 5

Euler Line, Euler Circle, and Locus Problems

Introduction

In this final lesson on homothety, we explore advanced applications including the famous Euler line and Euler circle of a triangle, as well as various locus problems. These topics demonstrate the power of homothety in establishing important geometric relationships and solving complex problems.

Through these exercises, you will learn how homothety can be used to prove alignment of special points in triangles, discover interesting circle properties, and determine paths traced by points under given conditions.

XIX. Euler Line ‚Äì Euler Circle

Activity 1

Given a triangle ABC, denote by O the circumcenter, G the centroid, and H the orthocenter of triangle ABC. Let A', B', and C' be the respective midpoints of [BC], [AC], and [AB]. Let h be the homothety with center G and ratio -1/2.

Figure: Triangle ABC with circumcenter O, centroid G, orthocenter H
Triangle ABC with circumcircle, centroid G, orthocenter H, and midpoints A', B', C'.

1) a) Make a figure.

b) Show that the image of altitude (AH) by homothety h is the perpendicular bisector of [BC].

Consider homothety h(G, -1/2):

  • A ‚Ü¶ A' (since A' is midpoint of BC and G divides median in ratio 2:1)
  • H ‚Ü¶ O (we will prove this in part 2)
  • Line AH ‚Ü¶ line through A' and O

But O is the circumcenter, so line A'O is the perpendicular bisector of BC.

h(G,-1/2): (AH) ‚Ü¶ (A'O) where (A'O) is perpendicular bisector of [BC]

c) Specify the images by h of altitudes (BH) and (CH).

Similarly:

  • h: (BH) ‚Ü¶ (B'O) where (B'O) is perpendicular bisector of [AC]
  • h: (CH) ‚Ü¶ (C'O) where (C'O) is perpendicular bisector of [AB]
h(G,-1/2): altitudes ‚Ü¶ perpendicular bisectors

2) Show that points O, G, and H are collinear and that OH = 3OG. (The line containing points O, G, and H is called the Euler line of triangle ABC)

From the homothety h(G, -1/2):

  • We have H ‚Ü¶ O (since altitudes ‚Ü¶ perpendicular bisectors)
  • By definition of homothety: \(\overrightarrow{GO} = -\frac{1}{2} \overrightarrow{GH}\)
  • This implies that O, G, H are collinear with G between O and H
  • From the equation: \(\overrightarrow{GO} = -\frac{1}{2} \overrightarrow{GH} \Rightarrow \overrightarrow{GH} = -2 \overrightarrow{GO}\)
  • Thus \(\overrightarrow{OH} = \overrightarrow{OG} + \overrightarrow{GH} = \overrightarrow{OG} - 2\overrightarrow{GO} = \overrightarrow{OG} + 2\overrightarrow{OG} = 3\overrightarrow{OG}\)
  • Therefore OH = 3OG
\(\overrightarrow{OH} = 3\overrightarrow{OG}\) ‚áí OH = 3OG and O, G, H are collinear
This line is called the Euler line of the triangle.

3) Let Œ© be the circumcenter of triangle A'B'C'. Show that Œ© belongs to line (OG) and that it is the midpoint of [OH]. Calculate the radius r of this circle in terms of the radius R of the circumcircle of ABC. (The circumcircle of A'B'C' is called the Euler circle or nine-point circle of triangle ABC)

Consider triangle A'B'C' (midpoint triangle of ABC):

  • A'B'C' is homothetic to ABC by homothety h‚ÇÅ with center G and ratio -1/2
  • The circumcircle of A'B'C' is the image of circumcircle of ABC by h‚ÇÅ
  • Therefore Œ© = h‚ÇÅ(O) = image of O by h(G, -1/2)
  • Since h maps O to Œ© and H to O, and h has ratio -1/2, we have:
    • \(\overrightarrow{GŒ©} = -\frac{1}{2} \overrightarrow{GO}\)
    • \(\overrightarrow{GH} = -2 \overrightarrow{GO}\) (from part 2)
  • Thus Œ© is the midpoint of OH because:
    • \(\overrightarrow{OŒ©} = \overrightarrow{OG} + \overrightarrow{GŒ©} = \overrightarrow{OG} - \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{GO} = \overrightarrow{OG} + \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{OG} = \frac{3}{2}\overrightarrow{OG}\)
    • \(\overrightarrow{OH} = 3\overrightarrow{OG}\) (from part 2)
    • So \(\overrightarrow{OŒ©} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{OH}\) ‚áí Œ© is midpoint of OH

For the radius:

  • Homothety h(G, -1/2) transforms circumcircle of ABC (radius R) to circumcircle of A'B'C'
  • Since the absolute ratio is 1/2, the radius r = (1/2)R
Œ© = midpoint of [OH], r = \(\frac{1}{2}\)R
This circle is called the Euler circle or nine-point circle of the triangle.
XX. Locus Problem A

Activity 2

Let C be a circle with center O and A a point not belonging to C. Let M be a point on circle C, and let I be the barycenter of points (A, 2) and (M, 3). Determine the locus of point I as point M describes circle C.

Figure: Circle C with point A outside, point M on C, and barycenter I
Circle C with center O, point A outside, point M moving on C, and barycenter I of (A,2) and (M,3).

I is barycenter of (A,2) and (M,3), so by definition:

\(2\overrightarrow{IA} + 3\overrightarrow{IM} = \overrightarrow{0}\) or equivalently \(\overrightarrow{AI} = \frac{3}{5}\overrightarrow{AM}\)

This means that I is the image of M by homothety h with center A and ratio 3/5:

I = h(A, 3/5)(M)

Since M describes circle C, and homothety transforms circles to circles:

  • The image of C by h(A, 3/5) is a circle C'
  • Center of C' = h(A, 3/5)(O) = O' such that \(\overrightarrow{AO'} = \frac{3}{5}\overrightarrow{AO}\)
  • Radius of C' = (3/5) √ó radius of C

Therefore, the locus of I is circle C' with center O' (on line AO at 3/5 of distance from A to O) and radius (3/5)R.

XXI. Locus Problem B

Activity 3

Let C be a circle with diameter [AB] and S a point on (AB). M being a point of C, we draw the diameter [PM], then the point M' intersection of lines (SM) and (AP).

Figure: Circle with diameter AB, point S on AB, point M on circle, diameter PM, and intersection M'
Circle C with diameter AB, point S on AB, point M on circle, diameter PM, intersection M' of SM and AP.

1) Let h be the homothety with center S that transforms B into A. Determine the image by h of line (BM).

Homothety h(S, k) with B ‚Ü¶ A:

\(\overrightarrow{SA} = k \overrightarrow{SB}\) ‚áí k = SA/SB

Under this homothety:

  • B ‚Ü¶ A
  • M ‚Ü¶ ?

Since P is diametrically opposite to M, and AP is a line through A and P:

  • Consider triangle SBM
  • Under h: S ‚Ü¶ S (fixed point), B ‚Ü¶ A, M ‚Ü¶ some point on line through A
  • But M' is intersection of SM and AP
  • Thus h(M) = M' (since M' is on line AP and on line through S and image of M)

Therefore h: (BM) ‚Ü¶ (AM') which is line through A and M'

2) Determine and construct the locus of point M' when M describes circle C.

From part 1, M' = h(M) where h is homothety with center S and ratio k = SA/SB.

As M describes circle C, its image M' describes the image of C by h.

Therefore, the locus of M' is a circle C' with:

  • Center O' = h(O) where O is center of C (midpoint of AB)
  • Radius r' = |k| √ó R where R is radius of C

Since S is on line AB, O' is also on line AB.

The circle C' is the image of C by homothety h(S, SA/SB).

XXII. Locus Problem C

Activity 4

Let C be a circle with center O and [AB] a chord of this circle. Let M be a variable point on circle C and N the point defined by MN = AB (with same direction).

Figure: Circle C with chord AB, point M on circle, point N such that MN = AB
Circle C with chord AB, point M moving on circle, point N such that vector MN = vector AB.

1) Determine the locus of point N when M describes circle C.

We have \(\overrightarrow{MN} = \overrightarrow{AB}\) constant.

Thus N is the image of M by translation with vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\).

\(\overrightarrow{ON} = \overrightarrow{OM} + \overrightarrow{MN} = \overrightarrow{OM} + \overrightarrow{AB}\)

Since M describes circle C with center O and radius R, and translation preserves circles:

  • N describes a circle C' with center O' = O + \(\overrightarrow{AB}\)
  • Radius of C' = R (same as C)

Therefore, the locus of N is circle C' with center O' and radius R, where O' is the translation of O by vector AB.

2) Let I be the midpoint of [AN]. What is the locus of point I when M describes circle C?

I is midpoint of AN, so:

\(\overrightarrow{AI} = \frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{AN} = \frac{1}{2}(\overrightarrow{AM} + \overrightarrow{MN}) = \frac{1}{2}(\overrightarrow{AM} + \overrightarrow{AB})\)

Alternatively, consider homothety h with center A and ratio 1/2:

I = h(A, 1/2)(N)

From part 1, N describes circle C' with center O' and radius R.

Under homothety h(A, 1/2):

  • Circle C' ‚Ü¶ circle C'' with center O'' = h(A, 1/2)(O')
  • Radius of C'' = (1/2)R

Therefore, the locus of I is circle C'' with center O'' and radius R/2.

3) Let G be the centroid of triangle BMN. Determine the locus of point G when M describes circle C.

G is centroid of triangle BMN, so:

\(\overrightarrow{G} = \frac{1}{3}(\overrightarrow{B} + \overrightarrow{M} + \overrightarrow{N})\)

But \(\overrightarrow{N} = \overrightarrow{M} + \overrightarrow{AB}\), so:

\(\overrightarrow{G} = \frac{1}{3}(\overrightarrow{B} + \overrightarrow{M} + (\overrightarrow{M} + \overrightarrow{AB})) = \frac{1}{3}(\overrightarrow{B} + 2\overrightarrow{M} + \overrightarrow{AB})\)

Simplify using \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{B} - \overrightarrow{A}\):

\(\overrightarrow{G} = \frac{1}{3}(2\overrightarrow{B} - \overrightarrow{A} + 2\overrightarrow{M}) = \frac{2}{3}\overrightarrow{M} + \frac{1}{3}(2\overrightarrow{B} - \overrightarrow{A})\)

This can be written as:

\(\overrightarrow{G} = \frac{2}{3}\overrightarrow{M} + \overrightarrow{C}\) where \(\overrightarrow{C} = \frac{1}{3}(2\overrightarrow{B} - \overrightarrow{A})\) is constant

Thus G is the image of M by homothety with center at the origin and ratio 2/3, followed by translation by vector C.

Since M describes circle C with center O and radius R:

  • After homothety with ratio 2/3: circle with center (2/3)O and radius (2/3)R
  • After translation by C: circle with center (2/3)O + C and radius (2/3)R

Therefore, the locus of G is a circle with center (2/3)O + (1/3)(2B - A) and radius (2/3)R.

Comprehensive Summary

Key Results Demonstrated

  • The Euler line: O, G, H are collinear with OH = 3OG
  • The Euler circle (nine-point circle): circumcircle of midpoint triangle with radius R/2 and center at midpoint of OH
  • Homothety transforms circles to circles, with center and radius scaled accordingly
  • Locus problems can be solved by identifying transformations (homothety, translation) that relate moving points
  • Barycenters and centroids can be expressed in terms of homotheties

Conclusion

This lesson concludes our exploration of homothety with advanced applications including the famous Euler line and Euler circle of a triangle, as well as various locus problems. These topics demonstrate the power of homothety in establishing important geometric relationships and solving complex problems. The Euler line shows how three significant points of a triangle are always aligned in a specific ratio, while the Euler circle reveals a beautiful relationship between the circumcircle and the midpoint triangle. Locus problems illustrate how homothety, combined with other transformations, can determine the paths of points under given conditions. Mastery of these concepts provides a strong foundation for advanced geometric reasoning and problem-solving.
