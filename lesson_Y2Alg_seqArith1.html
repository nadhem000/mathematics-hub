Arithmetic Sequences: Part I

Introduction to Number Patterns

Introduction

In this lesson, we will explore the concept of sequences. A sequence is an ordered list of numbers that follows a specific pattern or rule. Understanding sequences helps us recognize patterns in mathematics and in the world around us.

We will start by identifying patterns in given lists of numbers, then explore special sequences like triangular numbers, and finally learn the formal definition of a sequence.

I. Concept of Sequences

Activity 1

Each of the following lists of numbers follows a pattern. Discover this pattern and give the next two terms.

a) 5; 3; 1; -1; -3; ...

Let's examine the differences between consecutive terms:

From 5 to 3: 3 - 5 = -2

From 3 to 1: 1 - 3 = -2

From 1 to -1: -1 - 1 = -2

From -1 to -3: -3 - (-1) = -2

Each term is obtained by subtracting 2 from the previous term. This is called an arithmetic sequence with common difference -2.

To find the next two terms:

-3 - 2 = -5

-5 - 2 = -7

The complete sequence: 5; 3; 1; -1; -3; -5; -7

b) 1/4; 1; 7/4; 5/2; 13/4; ...

Let's write all terms with denominator 4 to see the pattern more clearly:

1/4 = 1/4

1 = 4/4

7/4 = 7/4

5/2 = 10/4

13/4 = 13/4

Now look at the numerators: 1, 4, 7, 10, 13, ...

The difference between consecutive numerators: 4-1=3, 7-4=3, 10-7=3, 13-10=3

Each numerator increases by 3. This is also an arithmetic sequence with common difference 3/4 (since we keep denominator 4).

To find the next two terms:

Next numerator: 13 + 3 = 16 ‚Üí 16/4 = 4

Following numerator: 16 + 3 = 19 ‚Üí 19/4 = 19/4

The complete sequence: 1/4; 1; 7/4; 5/2; 13/4; 4; 19/4

c) 1; 2; 3; 5; 8; ...

Let's examine how each term relates to the previous ones:

1 + 2 = 3 (third term equals sum of first two)

2 + 3 = 5 (fourth term equals sum of previous two)

3 + 5 = 8 (fifth term equals sum of previous two)

This is a Fibonacci-like sequence where each term (after the first two) is the sum of the two preceding terms.

To find the next two terms:

5 + 8 = 13

8 + 13 = 21

The complete sequence: 1; 2; 3; 5; 8; 13; 21

d) 1; 4; 9; 16; 25; ...

Let's check if these numbers are perfect squares:

1 = 1¬≤ = 1 √ó 1

4 = 2¬≤ = 2 √ó 2

9 = 3¬≤ = 3 √ó 3

16 = 4¬≤ = 4 √ó 4

25 = 5¬≤ = 5 √ó 5

These are square numbers - each term is the square of its position in the sequence (1st term is 1¬≤, 2nd term is 2¬≤, etc.).

To find the next two terms:

6¬≤ = 6 √ó 6 = 36

7¬≤ = 7 √ó 7 = 49

The complete sequence: 1; 4; 9; 16; 25; 36; 49

e) -2; -3; -5; -9; -17; ...

Let's examine the differences between consecutive terms:

-3 - (-2) = -1

-5 - (-3) = -2

-9 - (-5) = -4

-17 - (-9) = -8

The differences are: -1, -2, -4, -8, ...

Notice that each difference is double the previous difference: -2 = 2 √ó (-1), -4 = 2 √ó (-2), -8 = 2 √ó (-4)

So the pattern is: each term is obtained by subtracting a number that keeps doubling: subtract 1, then 2, then 4, then 8, ...

To find the next two terms:

Next difference: 2 √ó (-8) = -16

-17 + (-16) = -17 - 16 = -33

Following difference: 2 √ó (-16) = -32

-33 + (-32) = -33 - 32 = -65

The complete sequence: -2; -3; -5; -9; -17; -33; -65

c) Give the expression for the nth triangular number T‚Çô as a function of n.

From our observations:

T‚ÇÅ = 1 = (1 √ó 2) √∑ 2

T‚ÇÇ = 3 = (2 √ó 3) √∑ 2

We can see the pattern: T‚Çô = n √ó (n + 1) √∑ 2

\[T_n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\]

This formula works because when you put two triangular numbers together, you get a rectangle of dimensions n by (n+1), so one triangle is half of that rectangle.

Definition of a Sequence

Definition

Let n‚ÇÄ be a natural number. When to every natural number n greater than or equal to n‚ÇÄ we associate a unique real number U(n), we say that we have defined a sequence of real numbers.

The real number U(n) is called the general term of the sequence and is denoted U‚Çô. The sequence is denoted (U‚Çô)‚Çô ‚â• n‚ÇÄ or simply (U‚Çô).

Lesson Summary

Key Concepts Learned

  • A sequence is an ordered list of numbers that follows a specific pattern
  • We can find patterns by looking at differences between terms, ratios, or other relationships
  • Triangular numbers follow the formula T‚Çô = n(n+1)/2
  • A sequence is formally defined as a function that assigns a real number to each natural number
  • The general term U‚Çô gives us a formula to find any term in the sequence

Types of Sequences Encountered

  • Arithmetic sequences: Each term differs from the previous by a constant (like 5, 3, 1, -1, -3, ... with difference -2)
  • Fibonacci-like sequences: Each term is the sum of the two preceding terms (like 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ...)
  • Square numbers: Each term is a perfect square (like 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ...)
  • Triangular numbers: Each term represents dots arranged in a triangle (1, 3, 6, 10, 15, ...)
