1st Degree Equations I: Algebraic Manipulation

Introduction

First degree equations, also known as linear equations, are the foundation of algebra. They represent relationships where one variable changes at a constant rate relative to another.

Definition

A first degree equation is an equation where the highest power of the variable is 1. The general form is: ax + b = 0, where a and b are constants, and a â‰  0.

In this lesson, we'll learn how to solve these equations step by step, with plenty of examples and practice problems.

Solving 1st Degree Equations

Step 1: Simplify Both Sides

If there are parentheses, distribute to remove them. Combine like terms on each side of the equation.

Example: 3(x + 2) - 2x = 4

First, distribute the 3: 3x + 6 - 2x = 4

Then combine like terms: x + 6 = 4

Step 2: Isolate the Variable

Use inverse operations to move constants to the other side of the equation. If a number is added to the variable, subtract it from both sides. If a number is multiplied by the variable, divide both sides by that number.

Continuing from our example: x + 6 = 4

Subtract 6 from both sides: x + 6 - 6 = 4 - 6

Simplify: x = -2

Step 3: Check Your Solution

Substitute your solution back into the original equation to verify it works.

Check: 3(-2 + 2) - 2(-2) = 4

Simplify: 3(0) + 4 = 4

Which gives: 4 = 4 âœ“

Important Note

Whatever operation you perform on one side of the equation, you must perform the same operation on the other side to maintain equality.
