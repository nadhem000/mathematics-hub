Summary of Divisibility Rules

Divisibility by 2 and 5

  • â™¦ An integer is divisible by 2 (respectively by 5) if and only if its units digit is divisible by 2 (respectively by 5).
  • â™¦ The remainder of the Euclidean division of an integer by 2 (respectively by 5) is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division of its units digit by 2 (respectively by 5).

Divisibility by 4 and 25

  • â™¦ An integer is divisible by 4 (respectively by 25) if and only if the number formed by its last two digits is divisible by 4 (respectively by 25).
  • â™¦ The remainder of the Euclidean division of an integer by 4 (respectively by 25) is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division by 4 (respectively by 25) of the number formed by its last two digits.

Divisibility by 3 and 9

  • â™¦ An integer is divisible by 3 (respectively by 9) if and only if the sum of its digits is divisible by 3 (respectively by 9).
  • â™¦ The remainder of the Euclidean division of an integer by 3 (respectively by 9) is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division of the sum of its digits by 3 (respectively by 9).

Divisibility by 8

  • â™¦ An integer, greater than or equal to 100, is divisible by 8 if and only if the number formed by its last three digits is divisible by 8.
  • â™¦ The remainder of the Euclidean division of an integer by 8 is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division by 8 of the number formed by its last three digits.

Divisibility by 11

Let \(n\) be a natural integer. Let \(S_1\) be the sum of its digits in odd positions (from right to left) and \(S_2\) be the sum of its digits in even positions. Let \(d = S_1 - S_2\).

Case 1: \(d \geq 0\)

  • â™¦ \(n\) is divisible by 11 if and only if \(d\) is divisible by 11.
  • â™¦ The remainder of the Euclidean division of \(n\) by 11 is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division of \(d\) by 11.

Case 2: \(d < 0\)

  • â™¦ Let \(p\) be the smallest natural integer such that \(d + 11p\) is positive or zero.
  • â™¦ \(n\) is divisible by 11 if and only if \(d + 11p\) is divisible by 11.
  • â™¦ The remainder of the Euclidean division of \(n\) by 11 is \(d + 11p\).

Exercise 1: Cubic Boxes Problem

The dimensions of a rectangular box are 120 cm, 200 cm, and 180 cm. We want to create cubic boxes with the same edge length \(a\) (where \(a\) is an integer) that completely fill the rectangular box. What should be the edge length \(a\) to use the minimum number of boxes?

To completely fill the rectangular box with identical cubes, the edge length \(a\) must divide all three dimensions of the box.

So \(a\) must be a common divisor of 120, 200, and 180.

To use the minimum number of boxes, we need to maximize the volume of each cube, which means we need the largest possible \(a\).

Therefore, \(a\) must be the greatest common divisor (GCD) of 120, 200, and 180.

Let's find the GCD:

Prime factorization: \(120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5\)
\(200 = 2^3 \times 5^2\)
\(180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5\)
The common factors are: \(2^2\) and \(5^1\)
GCD = \(2^2 \times 5 = 4 \times 5 = 20\)

Therefore, \(a = 20\) cm.

Number of boxes: \(\frac{120}{20} \times \frac{200}{20} \times \frac{180}{20} = 6 \times 10 \times 9 = 540\) boxes.

Exercise 2: Four-Digit Number with Consecutive Digits

Let \(n\) be a natural integer with 4 digits such that:

  • \(n\) is divisible by 9
  • The digits of \(n\) are consecutive and arranged in increasing order from left to right.

Find \(n\).

Let the digits be \(a, a+1, a+2, a+3\) where \(a\) is a digit from 0 to 6 (since we need 4 consecutive digits).

The number is: \(1000a + 100(a+1) + 10(a+2) + (a+3) = 1111a + 123\)

Sum of digits: \(a + (a+1) + (a+2) + (a+3) = 4a + 6\)

For divisibility by 9, the sum of digits must be divisible by 9:

\(4a + 6\) must be divisible by 9

Since \(a\) is a digit from 0 to 6, let's check possible values:

For \(a = 0\): \(4(0) + 6 = 6\) (not divisible by 9)
For \(a = 1\): \(4(1) + 6 = 10\) (not divisible by 9)
For \(a = 2\): \(4(2) + 6 = 14\) (not divisible by 9)
For \(a = 3\): \(4(3) + 6 = 18\) (divisible by 9 âœ“)
For \(a = 4\): \(4(4) + 6 = 22\) (not divisible by 9)
For \(a = 5\): \(4(5) + 6 = 26\) (not divisible by 9)
For \(a = 6\): \(4(6) + 6 = 30\) (not divisible by 9)

Only \(a = 3\) works.

The digits are: 3, 4, 5, 6
The number is: \(3456\)

Check: \(3456 \div 9 = 384\) âœ“

Exercise 3: Integer Quotient

Find all natural integers \(n\) such that \(\frac{n+25}{n+4}\) is an integer.

We need \(\frac{n+25}{n+4}\) to be an integer.

Let \(k = \frac{n+25}{n+4}\) where \(k\) is an integer.
Then \(n+25 = k(n+4)\)
\(n+25 = kn + 4k\)
\(n - kn = 4k - 25\)
\(n(1-k) = 4k - 25\)
\(n = \frac{4k - 25}{1-k}\) (for \(k \neq 1\))
\(n = \frac{4k - 25}{1-k} = -\frac{4k - 25}{k-1} = -\frac{4k - 4 - 21}{k-1} = -\frac{4(k-1) - 21}{k-1} = -4 + \frac{21}{k-1}\)

So \(n = -4 + \frac{21}{k-1}\)

For \(n\) to be a natural number, \(\frac{21}{k-1}\) must be an integer and \(n \geq 0\).

The divisors of 21 are: \(\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm 7, \pm 21\).

Let \(d = k-1\). Then \(d\) must divide 21.

Case by case:

If \(d = 1\): \(k = 2\), \(n = -4 + 21 = 17\) âœ“
If \(d = 3\): \(k = 4\), \(n = -4 + 7 = 3\) âœ“
If \(d = 7\): \(k = 8\), \(n = -4 + 3 = -1\) âœ— (not natural)
If \(d = 21\): \(k = 22\), \(n = -4 + 1 = -3\) âœ—
Negative divisors give negative \(n\) values.

Therefore, the natural numbers \(n\) are: 3 and 17.

Check: For \(n = 3\): \(\frac{3+25}{3+4} = \frac{28}{7} = 4\) âœ“

For \(n = 17\): \(\frac{17+25}{17+4} = \frac{42}{21} = 2\) âœ“

Exercise 4: Divisible Difference

Determine the values of the natural integer \(p\) such that \(p - 1\) divides \(p + 11\).

We need \(p - 1\) to divide \(p + 11\), which means \(\frac{p+11}{p-1}\) must be an integer.

Let \(k = \frac{p+11}{p-1}\) where \(k\) is an integer.
Then \(p+11 = k(p-1)\)
\(p+11 = kp - k\)
\(p - kp = -k - 11\)
\(p(1-k) = -k - 11\)
\(p = \frac{-k - 11}{1-k} = \frac{k+11}{k-1}\) (for \(k \neq 1\))

Rewrite: \(p = \frac{k+11}{k-1} = \frac{k-1+12}{k-1} = 1 + \frac{12}{k-1}\)

For \(p\) to be a natural number, \(\frac{12}{k-1}\) must be an integer and \(p \geq 0\).

The divisors of 12 are: \(\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6, \pm 12\).

Let \(d = k-1\). Then \(d\) must divide 12.

Case by case (only considering positive divisors for positive \(p\)):

If \(d = 1\): \(k = 2\), \(p = 1 + 12 = 13\) âœ“
If \(d = 2\): \(k = 3\), \(p = 1 + 6 = 7\) âœ“
If \(d = 3\): \(k = 4\), \(p = 1 + 4 = 5\) âœ“
If \(d = 4\): \(k = 5\), \(p = 1 + 3 = 4\) âœ“
If \(d = 6\): \(k = 7\), \(p = 1 + 2 = 3\) âœ“
If \(d = 12\): \(k = 13\), \(p = 1 + 1 = 2\) âœ“

Also consider negative divisors that give positive \(p\):

If \(d = -1\): \(k = 0\), \(p = 1 - 12 = -11\) âœ—
If \(d = -2\): \(k = -1\), \(p = 1 - 6 = -5\) âœ—
If \(d = -3\): \(k = -2\), \(p = 1 - 4 = -3\) âœ—
If \(d = -4\): \(k = -3\), \(p = 1 - 3 = -2\) âœ—
If \(d = -6\): \(k = -5\), \(p = 1 - 2 = -1\) âœ—
If \(d = -12\): \(k = -11\), \(p = 1 - 1 = 0\) âœ— (0 is not usually considered a natural number in this context)

Therefore, the natural numbers \(p\) are: 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 13.

Check: For \(p = 2\): \(p-1 = 1\), \(p+11 = 13\), 1 divides 13 âœ“

For \(p = 3\): \(3-1 = 2\), \(3+11 = 14\), 2 divides 14 âœ“

For \(p = 4\): \(4-1 = 3\), \(4+11 = 15\), 3 divides 15 âœ“

For \(p = 5\): \(5-1 = 4\), \(5+11 = 16\), 4 divides 16 âœ“

For \(p = 7\): \(7-1 = 6\), \(7+11 = 18\), 6 divides 18 âœ“
