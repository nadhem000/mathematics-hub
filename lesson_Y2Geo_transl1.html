Translation: Part 1

Translation and Planar Transformations

Introduction

Transformations of the plane, including translations, symmetries, and rotations, are fundamental concepts in geometry. They allow us to understand how shapes move and relate to each other in space.

In this lesson, we will explore the concept of applications from the plane to itself, study translations in depth, and learn how to construct and analyze geometric transformations.

Initial Problem: Parallelograms Analysis

In the opposite figure, ACBD and CEDF are two parallelograms. Among the proposed statements, one or more are correct. Which ones?

Figure: Two Parallelograms ACBD and CEDF
Points A, B, C, D form parallelogram ACBD. Points C, E, D, F form parallelogram CEDF. Point E is inside quadrilateral AEDF.

Statements to Analyze

a) The axial symmetry with axis (DC) sends A to B and E to F.

TRUE. In parallelogram ACBD, (DC) is the perpendicular bisector of [AB]. Also, in parallelogram CEDF, (DC) is the perpendicular bisector of [EF]. Therefore, the axial symmetry with axis (DC) sends A to B and E to F.

b) The central symmetry with center at the midpoint of segment [DC] sends A to B, C to D and E to F.

TRUE. The midpoint of [DC] is the center of both parallelograms. For parallelogram ACBD, this point is the midpoint of both diagonals [AB] and [CD]. Similarly for parallelogram CEDF, it's the midpoint of [EF] and [CD]. Therefore, the central symmetry with this center sends A to B, C to D, and E to F.

c) The translation with vector AD sends C to B and E to F.

FALSE. Let's analyze: The translation with vector AD sends point A to D. In parallelogram ACBD, we have AD = BC (opposite sides are equal and parallel). So the translation with vector AD is equivalent to the translation with vector BC. This translation sends C to B (since C + BC = B). However, for point E: In parallelogram CEDF, we have CE = DF and CE âˆ¥ DF. The translation with vector AD sends C to B, but this doesn't necessarily send E to F. Actually, the translation sending C to B has vector CB, which is not equal to EF. Therefore, the statement is false.

Part II: Translations

A translation is a transformation that moves every point of the plane by the same vector.

Let \(\vec{u}\) be a vector. The translation of vector \(\vec{u}\) is denoted \(t_{\vec{u}}\):
\(t_{\vec{u}}: P \rightarrow P\)
\(M \mapsto M' = t_{\vec{u}}(M)\) such that \(\overrightarrow{MM'} = \vec{u}\)

Comprehensive Summary

Notion of Application of the Plane in Itself

When we associate to each point M of the plane exactly one point M', we define an application (transformation) of the plane into itself.

f: P â†’ P
M â†¦ M' = f(M)

M' is called the image of point M by the application f.
M is called an antecedent (preimage) of point M' by the application f.

Translations

Let \(\vec{u}\) be a vector. The application of the plane into itself which associates to each point M the unique point M' such that \(\overrightarrow{MM'} = \vec{u}\) is called the translation of vector \(\vec{u}\). It is denoted \(t_{\vec{u}}\).

\(t_{\vec{u}}: P \rightarrow P\)
\(M \mapsto t_{\vec{u}}(M) = M'\)

M' is the image of M by \(t_{\vec{u}}\).

\(t_{\vec{u}}(M) = M' \iff \overrightarrow{MM'} = \vec{u}\)

Key Property

The composition of two central symmetries with centers I and J is a translation of vector \(2\overrightarrow{IJ}\):

\(S_J \circ S_I = t_{2\overrightarrow{IJ}}\)
