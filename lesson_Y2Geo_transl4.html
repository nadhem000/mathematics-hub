Translation: Part 4

Image of a Circle and Conservation of Contact

Introduction

In this lesson, we will study how translations affect circles and tangency. We will prove that the image of a circle under translation is another congruent circle, and that translations preserve tangency between circles and lines.

These properties are essential for understanding how geometric figures behave under transformations and have important applications in problem-solving.

Part X: Image of a Circle under Translation

A translation transforms a circle into another circle with the same radius. The center of the image circle is the image of the original center.

Activity 1: Image of a Circle

Figure: Circle C and its image C' under translation t
Circle C with center O is translated by vector u to circle C' with center O'. Points M and N' demonstrate the correspondence between points on the circles.

1) Let t be a translation of vector \(\vec{u}\), C a circle with center O and radius R, and O' = t(O) the image of O under t. Let C' be the circle with center O' and radius R.

Let M be a point on C and M' = t(M). Show that M' belongs to C'.

Since M is on circle C, we have:

\(OM = R\)

By definition of translation t:

\(\overrightarrow{OO'} = \vec{u}\) and \(\overrightarrow{MM'} = \vec{u}\)

Therefore:

\(\overrightarrow{O'M'} = \overrightarrow{O'O} + \overrightarrow{OM} + \overrightarrow{MM'}\)
\(\overrightarrow{O'M'} = -\vec{u} + \overrightarrow{OM} + \vec{u} = \overrightarrow{OM}\)

Thus \(O'M' = OM = R\), which means M' is on circle C'.

2) Let N' be a point on C' and N the antecedent of N' by t (i.e., N = tâ»Â¹(N')). Show that N belongs to C.

Since N' is on circle C', we have:

\(O'N' = R\)

By definition of translation, N = tâ»Â¹(N') means that t(N) = N', so:

\(\overrightarrow{NN'} = \vec{u}\)

Also, since O' = t(O), we have \(\overrightarrow{OO'} = \vec{u}\). Therefore:

\(\overrightarrow{ON} = \overrightarrow{OO'} + \overrightarrow{O'N'}\)? Wait, let's calculate carefully:
\(\overrightarrow{ON} = \overrightarrow{OO'} + \overrightarrow{O'N'} + \overrightarrow{N'N}\)
\(\overrightarrow{ON} = \vec{u} + \overrightarrow{O'N'} - \vec{u} = \overrightarrow{O'N'}\)

Thus \(ON = O'N' = R\), which means N is on circle C.

3) What can we conclude?

From parts (1) and (2), we can conclude:

  • Every point on circle C has its image on circle C'
  • Every point on circle C' has its antecedent on circle C

This establishes a bijection between the points of C and C' under the translation t. Therefore, the image of circle C under translation t is exactly circle C'.

If C is a circle with center O and radius R, and t is a translation of vector \(\vec{u}\), then:
\(t(C) = C'\) where C' is the circle with center \(O' = t(O)\) and radius R.

Part XI: Conservation of Contact under Translation

Translations preserve tangency: if a line is tangent to a circle, then the image of the line under translation is tangent to the image of the circle.

Activity 2: Conservation of Tangency

Figure: Circle C with tangent line Î” at point M, and their images under translation
Circle C with tangent line Î” at point M. Under translation t, they become circle C' and line Î”' tangent at point M'.

Let C be a circle and Î” a line tangent to C at point M. Let t be a translation. We denote by C', Î”', and M' the respective images of C, Î”, and M under t.

Show that Î”' is tangent to C' at point M'.

We need to show two things:

  1. M' is on both C' and Î”'
  2. Î”' is tangent to C' at M'

1) Since M is on C, by Activity 1, M' = t(M) is on C'. Also, since M is on Î”, and Î”' = t(Î”) is the image of Î” under translation, M' is on Î”'.

2) To show Î”' is tangent to C' at M', we need to show that Î”' is perpendicular to the radius O'M' at M'.

Since Î” is tangent to C at M, Î” is perpendicular to radius OM at M.

Let \(\vec{v}\) be a direction vector of Î”. Since Î” âŸ‚ OM at M, we have:

\(\vec{v} \cdot \overrightarrow{OM} = 0\) (dot product)

Under translation t with vector \(\vec{u}\):

\(\overrightarrow{O'M'} = \overrightarrow{OM}\) (as shown in Activity 1)
\(\vec{v}' = \vec{v}\) (translations preserve direction vectors)

Therefore:

\(\vec{v}' \cdot \overrightarrow{O'M'} = \vec{v} \cdot \overrightarrow{OM} = 0\)

This shows that Î”' is perpendicular to O'M' at M', so Î”' is tangent to C' at M'.

Thus, translations preserve tangency between circles and lines.

Comprehensive Summary

Image of a Circle under Translation

Let t be a translation of vector \(\vec{u}\), and let C be a circle with center O and radius R. The image of C under t is:

\(t(C) = C'\) where C' is the circle with center \(O' = t(O)\) and radius R.

The translation establishes a bijection between the points of C and C'. For any point M on C, M' = t(M) is on C', and for any point N' on C', N = tâ»Â¹(N') is on C.

Conservation of Tangency

Translations preserve tangency between circles and lines. If a line Î” is tangent to a circle C at point M, then under any translation t:

The image line Î”' = t(Î”) is tangent to the image circle C' = t(C) at point M' = t(M).

This property follows from the fact that translations preserve perpendicularity and distances.

General Consequences

  • Translations preserve the shape and size of circles (they are isometric transformations)
  • The image of a circle under translation is a congruent circle
  • Tangency relations are preserved under translations
  • These properties make translations useful in geometric constructions and proofs
