Arithmetic: Part 3

Divisibility Criteria by 3 and 9

Introduction

In this lesson, we explore divisibility criteria by 3 and 9. Unlike criteria based on the last digits, these rely on the sum of all digits of a number.

These criteria are particularly useful for checking divisibility of large numbers and have interesting applications in problem-solving.

Summary: Divisibility by 3 and 9

Divisibility by 3 and 9

â€¢ An integer is divisible by 3 (respectively by 9) if and only if the sum of its digits is divisible by 3 (respectively by 9).

â€¢ The remainder of the Euclidean division of an integer by 3 (respectively by 9) is equal to the remainder of the Euclidean division of the sum of its digits by 3 (respectively by 9).

Example: 123 is divisible by 3 because 1+2+3=6 is divisible by 3.

Example: 234 is divisible by 9 because 2+3+4=9 is divisible by 9.

Exercise 4: Proof

Prove that the difference between a number and the sum of its digits is always divisible by 9.

Let n be a number with digits \(a_k a_{k-1} ... a_1 a_0\).

\(n = a_k \times 10^k + a_{k-1} \times 10^{k-1} + ... + a_1 \times 10 + a_0\)

Sum of digits S = \(a_k + a_{k-1} + ... + a_1 + a_0\)

Now, \(10^m \equiv 1 \pmod{9}\) for any m â‰¥ 0 because \(10 = 9 + 1\).

Therefore, \(n \equiv a_k + a_{k-1} + ... + a_1 + a_0 \equiv S \pmod{9}\)

Thus, \(n - S \equiv 0 \pmod{9}\), so n - S is divisible by 9.
