Translation: Part 5

Locus Problems and Construction Problems Involving Translations

Introduction

In this fifth lesson on translations, we will explore two important types of geometric problems: locus problems and construction problems. These problems test our understanding of geometric configurations and transformations.

Locus problems ask us to determine the set of points satisfying certain geometric conditions, while construction problems challenge us to build geometric figures with specific properties using only a straightedge and compass.

Part XII: Problems of Loci

A locus is the set of all points satisfying certain geometric conditions. Locus problems often involve determining the path traced by a point as other elements in the configuration vary.

Activity 1: Locus Determined by Perpendicular Lines

Figure: Perpendicular lines D and D' intersecting at O, point A, and variable point M on D
Two perpendicular lines D and D' intersect at O. Point A is fixed. M varies on D. N is determined by constructing perpendicular from A to AM meeting D'. I is the midpoint of MN.

Step 1: Experimental Phase

Consider two perpendicular lines D and D' intersecting at point O, and a point A not on either line. Let M be a variable point on line D. The perpendicular to line (AM) at point A meets line D' at point N. Let I be the midpoint of segment [MN].

a) Construct a figure.
b) Identify fixed and variable elements.
c) Place several points I corresponding to different positions of M.

Step 2: Conjecture

Make a conjecture about the set of points I as M varies along line D.

Based on experimental observation, it appears that point I traces a straight line parallel to line D and passing through the midpoint of segment [OA].

Step 3: Proof of Conjecture

Prove that I varies along a specific line.

Let O be the origin of a coordinate system with axes along D and D'. Without loss of generality, let:

\(D: y = 0\) (horizontal axis)
\(D': x = 0\) (vertical axis)

Let A have coordinates \((a, b)\) with \(a â‰  0\) and \(b â‰  0\). Let M have coordinates \((m, 0)\) where m is variable.

The slope of line AM is:

\(slope(AM) = \frac{0 - b}{m - a} = \frac{-b}{m - a}\)

The perpendicular at A has slope:

\(slope_{\perp} = \frac{m - a}{b}\)

The equation of the perpendicular through A is:

\(y - b = \frac{m - a}{b}(x - a)\)

This line intersects D' (x = 0) at N:

\(y_N - b = \frac{m - a}{b}(0 - a) = -\frac{a(m - a)}{b}\)
\(y_N = b - \frac{a(m - a)}{b} = \frac{b^2 - a(m - a)}{b}\)

Thus N has coordinates \((0, \frac{b^2 - a(m - a)}{b})\).

I is the midpoint of M(m, 0) and N(0, y_N):

\(I\left(\frac{m + 0}{2}, \frac{0 + y_N}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{m}{2}, \frac{y_N}{2}\right)\)
\(y_I = \frac{y_N}{2} = \frac{b^2 - a(m - a)}{2b}\)

From \(x_I = \frac{m}{2}\), we have \(m = 2x_I\). Substituting:

\(y_I = \frac{b^2 - a(2x_I - a)}{2b} = \frac{b^2 - 2a x_I + a^2}{2b}\)
\(y_I = -\frac{a}{b}x_I + \frac{a^2 + b^2}{2b}\)

This is the equation of a straight line with slope \(-\frac{a}{b}\) and y-intercept \(\frac{a^2 + b^2}{2b}\).

Therefore, as M varies on D, point I moves along a straight line.

Activity 2: Locus Determined by Parallelogram Construction

Figure: Points A and B, line D through A, and parallelogram ABNM
Points A and B are fixed. Line D passes through A. M varies on D. N completes parallelogram ABNM. The locus of N is shown.

Given points A and B and a line D passing through A but not through B. Let M be a variable point on D distinct from A. Let N be the point such that ABNM is a parallelogram.

a) Prove that point N is the image of point M under a specific transformation.

Since ABNM is a parallelogram, we have:

\(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{NM}\)

This can be rewritten as:

\(\overrightarrow{AN} = \overrightarrow{BM}\)

Also, from the parallelogram property:

\(\overrightarrow{AM} = \overrightarrow{BN}\)

Consider the translation t with vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\). Then:

\(t(A) = B\) and \(t(M) = N\)

Thus N is the image of M under the translation of vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\).

b) What is the locus of points N as M varies on D? Construct this locus.

Since N = t(M) where t is translation by vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\), and M varies on line D:

The locus of N is \(t(D)\)

Since translations map lines to parallel lines:

\(t(D)\) is parallel to D

Specifically, t(D) is the line through point B (since t(A) = B) parallel to D.

Therefore, the locus of N is the line through B parallel to D.

c) What is the locus of points N when M varies on a fixed circle C with center A and radius 3? Construct this locus.

Let C be the circle with center A and radius 3. Since N = t(M) where t is translation by vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\):

The locus of N is \(t(C)\)

We know from previous lessons that the image of a circle under translation is a congruent circle. Specifically:

\(t(C)\) is the circle with center \(t(A) = B\) and radius 3

Therefore, the locus of N is the circle with center B and radius 3.

Part XIII: Construction Problems

Construction problems involve creating geometric figures with specified properties using only straightedge and compass. These problems often require careful analysis and application of geometric theorems.

Activity 3: Constructing a Triangle from Midpoints

Figure: Triangle ABC with midpoints I, J, K of sides [BC], [AC], [AB] respectively
Given three non-collinear points I, J, K, construct triangle ABC such that I, J, K are midpoints of its sides.

Step 1: Analysis from a Study Figure

Given three non-collinear points I, J, K, we want to construct triangle ABC such that I, J, K are midpoints of [BC], [AC], and [AB] respectively.

a) Construct triangle ABC and place points I, J, K as midpoints.
b) Observe and prove that:

In triangle ABC with midpoints I, J, K:

  • IJ joins midpoints of BC and AC, so by the Midsegment Theorem, IJ âˆ¥ AB and IJ = Â½ AB
  • IK joins midpoints of BC and AB, so IK âˆ¥ AC and IK = Â½ AC
  • JK joins midpoints of AC and AB, so JK âˆ¥ BC and JK = Â½ BC

Thus each side of triangle ABC is parallel to the corresponding side of triangle IJK.

Step 2: Synthesis and Return to Initial Data

Given three non-collinear points I, J, K:

a) Construct the parallel to (IJ) through K and the parallel to (IK) through J.
b) Are these lines always intersecting? If yes, let A be their intersection.
c) Complete the construction of triangle ABC.

Construction Steps:

  1. Draw line through K parallel to IJ
  2. Draw line through J parallel to IK
  3. These lines intersect at point A (since I, J, K are non-collinear, the lines are not parallel)
  4. Construct point B such that K is midpoint of AB: B is symmetric to A with respect to K
  5. Construct point C such that J is midpoint of AC: C is symmetric to A with respect to J
  6. Verify that I is midpoint of BC

The construction is always possible when I, J, K are non-collinear.

Activity 4: Constructing a Parallelogram with Vertices on Given Lines

Figure: Intersecting lines D and Î”, points A and B, and parallelogram ABMN with M on D and N on Î”
Given intersecting lines D and Î”, and points A and B not on these lines, construct points M on D and N on Î” such that ABMN is a parallelogram.

Step 1: Analysis from a Study Figure

Given intersecting lines D and Î”, and distinct points A and B not on these lines. We want to construct points M on D and N on Î” such that ABMN is a parallelogram.

a) Construct parallelogram ABMN and intersecting lines D and Î” through M and N respectively.
b) Find a transformation t that maps M to N.
c) Let D' be the image of line D under t. Determine the intersection of D' and Î”.

Analysis:

  • Since ABMN is a parallelogram, we have \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{MN}\)
  • Thus N is the image of M under translation t with vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\)
  • D' = t(D) is parallel to D
  • N is on both D' and Î”, so N = D' âˆ© Î”

Therefore, N can be found as the intersection of Î” and the line through B parallel to D.

Step 2: Synthesis and Return to Initial Data

Given intersecting lines D and Î”, and distinct points A and B not on D or Î”:

a) Construct lines D and Î” and points A and B.
b) Construct line D' = t(D), the image of D under translation t with vector \(\overrightarrow{AB}\).
c) Show that D' and Î” intersect. Let N be their intersection.
d) Construct point M on line D such that ABMN is a parallelogram.

Construction Steps:

  1. Draw line through B parallel to D (this is D')
  2. Find N = intersection of D' and Î”
  3. Construct M such that \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{MN}\)
  4. Specifically, M can be found by translating N by vector \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) (opposite to \(\overrightarrow{AB}\))
  5. Verify that M lies on D and that ABMN is a parallelogram

The construction always works when D' and Î” intersect, which occurs unless D' is parallel to Î” (which would mean D is parallel to Î”, contradicting that D and Î” are intersecting).

Comprehensive Summary

Problems of Loci

  • A locus is the set of points satisfying certain geometric conditions
  • Loci can be lines, circles, or other curves
  • Translations transform loci in predictable ways

Construction Problems

  • Construction problems require creating figures with specified properties
  • Analysis involves working backward from a hypothetical solution
  • Synthesis involves using the analysis to create an actual construction

Key Translation Applications

  • Translations map lines to parallel lines
  • Translations map circles to congruent circles
  • Parallelograms can be constructed using translations
  • Midpoints provide information about parallel lines

Conclusion

Key Concepts Mastered

  • Understanding and solving locus problems
  • Using translations to determine loci
  • Constructing triangles from midpoints
  • Constructing parallelograms with vertices on given lines
  • Applying the Midsegment Theorem in constructions
  • Using analysis-synthesis approach in construction problems
  • Translating lines and circles to solve geometric problems

The problems studied in this lesson demonstrate the power of translations in solving both locus problems and construction problems. By understanding how translations transform geometric figures and applying this knowledge systematically, we can solve complex geometric problems that might otherwise seem intractable. These skills are essential for advanced geometry and have applications in fields such as engineering, computer graphics, and architecture.
